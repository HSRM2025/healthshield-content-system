# `market-survey-whitepaper` — SKILL.md

## Purpose

Use this skill to create a **market-facing, gated, survey-led whitepaper** for Health Shield that:

- presents survey findings in a clear, credible, journalist-friendly way
- supports PR coverage and wider campaign activity
- turns validated data into a strong editorial narrative
- balances insight, warmth, and authority
- reads as if a thoughtful person wrote it, not as if sections were assembled from a kit
- stays within compliance, brand, and regulatory boundaries

This skill is for whitepapers built around **survey results** rather than opinion alone. The output should feel polished, useful, and easy to quote, while staying careful with claims.

It should help employers, partners, brokers, media contacts, and other external audiences understand the story behind the findings, without drifting into hype or hard sell.

---

## How to use the published report as a guide

We have a published Health Shield survey whitepaper you can look at. Use it carefully, and use it for the right things.

**Use the published report as a guide for:**

- overall structure
- section order
- level of detail
- where expert commentary sits
- attribution and end-matter
- the general shape of a gated, PR-ready asset

**Do not copy from the published report:**

- its sentence rhythms
- its signposting habits
- its recurring formulas and stock phrases
- the way it lands the same kind of point in the same kind of way

In other words, mirror the **shape and quality**, not the **voice and cadence**. Aim for a plainer, more naturally written style. If anything, lean towards writing that feels composed with care rather than polished to a template. A report that reads as a little uneven and human is better than one that reads as smooth and machine-made.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> The published report is a structural reference, not a style template. If your draft starts to echo its phrasing or repeat its patterns, step back and vary it.

## Human and commercial significance

Not every finding needs a business case. This is one of the most important habits to get right.

Some findings matter most because of what they mean for real people facing real pressures. When that is the truer point, lead with the human significance and let it stand. You do not need to follow it with a line about productivity, absence, or retention every time.

Save the commercial framing – the workforce and business angle – for the findings where it genuinely fits. Forcing a business implication onto every section makes the report feel repetitive, and it quietly removes the warmth that makes people want to read on.

A simple rule of thumb:

- if a finding is about how someone copes day to day, **let it be human**
- if a finding clearly speaks to how an organisation runs, **the business angle is fair**
- if you are reaching to add a business point, **you probably do not need it**

## Avoiding formulaic and mechanically assembled writing

We want these reports to read like they were written, not built. A few clear habits help.

### Vary your openings

Sections and chapters should not all begin the same way. A chapter can open with:

- a single finding
- a short piece of context
- a plain statement
- a brief human detail

Avoid opening every chapter with the same run-up, such as “The findings suggest” or “The survey shows”.

### Vary your endings

Not every section needs a summary, a takeaway, or a business case. Some findings are strong enough to simply sit there. Let some sections close quietly.

### Vary your phrasing

If you have used a phrase once or twice, find a fresh way in next time. Watch especially for these, which sound templated when repeated:

- **“By X, organisations can Y”** — use no more than once in a report
- **“not just X, but Y”** and “not only… but also…” — use no more than once in a report
- **“This matters because…”** — vary your bridges from evidence to meaning
- **“What this shows is…”** — sometimes let the finding speak for itself

### Vary your chapter shapes

Do not build every chapter to the same challenge–implication–response pattern. Let at least one or two sections break the mould, opening differently or closing without a neat lesson.

### Use observation boxes sparingly

A short observation can help after a strong cluster of findings. It should not appear after every chapter. A summary box at the end of each section is one of the clearest signs of assembly-line writing.

> [!TIP]
> A good test: read three sections aloud at random. If they sound built from the same parts, vary them until they sound like something a person would actually say.

## Decision logic

Use this logic before and during drafting.

### 1. Is there a real story here?

If the data does not support a strong, useful narrative, do not force one. Build the report around what is clearly supported.

### 2. What is most newsworthy?

Prioritise findings that are:

- surprising
- timely
- relevant to current public or employer concerns
- strongly evidenced in the survey
- easy to express in one line
- likely to matter to media and stakeholders

### 3. What is most useful to the reader?

A media-friendly stat is helpful, but the report also needs real meaning. Choose findings that serve both external interest and reader value.

### 4. Is this point human, commercial, or both?

Before you write the meaning of a finding, decide whether the truer point is human, commercial, or both. Lead with whichever is more honest, and resist adding a business angle that is not really there.

### 5. What belongs in the main narrative?

Use the main chapters for findings that support the central story. Move lower-priority stats into:

- sidebars
- charts
- callouts
- supporting commentary

### 6. When should the brand appear?

Brand should support the insight story, not lead it. Mention Health Shield:

- in the introduction where it fits
- in expert commentary attribution
- in the conclusion
- in About Health Shield
- where context calls for it

Do not let the whitepaper become a sales document.

### 7. What should be quotable?

Prioritise lines that:

- sum up a clear finding
- connect evidence to real meaning
- sound natural when lifted into coverage
- stay accurate when quoted out of context

### 8. What must be checked again?

Always re-check:

- percentages
- year-on-year comparisons
- subgroup claims
- causality wording
- representativeness wording
- implied recommendations
- any sentence that sounds too absolute
- any phrase or shape you have already used more than twice

## Workflow

## 1. Confirm the brief

Start by checking:

- campaign objective
- target audience
- desired PR value
- gating context
- timeline
- review route
- required end matter
- whether year-on-year comparison is needed

**Goal:** know what the whitepaper needs to achieve before choosing angles.

---

## 2. Review and validate the research inputs

Read and organise:

- methodology
- topline findings
- cross-tabs
- subgroup cuts
- prior-year comparisons if available
- supporting commentary inputs

Flag:

- weak findings
- findings that need context
- findings that may be easily overstated
- findings that are interesting but not useful editorially

**Goal:** separate the strongest story material from background data.

---

## 3. Select the strongest media-worthy findings

Choose findings that are:

- clear
- relevant
- timely
- evidence-backed
- simple to repeat accurately
- meaningful to the audience

Look for a balanced mix of:

- headline figures
- supporting evidence
- subgroup nuance
- trend or comparison points
- human impact and, where it fits, business impact

**Do not** include stats just because they are available.

---

## 4. Build the narrative arc

Turn the chosen findings into a coherent journey.

A survey whitepaper often moves through the big picture, the pressure points, the consequences, what people need, and a measured close. This is a useful guide, not a template. Vary the shape of your chapters, and let some sections simply describe a finding without forcing a response.

### Narrative arc questions

- What is the central message?
- What tension or gap does the data reveal?
- Which chapters best support that message?
- What order helps the reader understand the issue clearly?
- Where should expert voices add depth?
- Where can a section breathe, rather than build to a lesson?

**Goal:** a report that reads as a connected piece of writing, not a stack of survey slides.

---

## 5. Create the chapter plan

Draft a chapter-by-chapter structure before full writing.

For each chapter, note:

- working heading
- main takeaway
- supporting stats
- subgroup insights
- chart or graphic opportunities
- possible quotable lines
- whether expert commentary belongs there
- whether the point is human, commercial, or both
- how this chapter will open and close differently from the last

### Example chapter purposes

| Chapter type | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| State of the issue | Set context and scale |
| Pressure point | Show what is driving concern |
| Human or business impact | Explain why the finding matters |
| What people need | Move from problem to response |
| Final thoughts | Close with calm authority and relevance |

---

## 6. Draft the executive summary early, refine late

Draft an early version to clarify direction. Then refine it once the body copy is complete.

The executive summary should:

- capture the biggest findings
- explain why they matter
- reflect the campaign objective
- stay quotable
- avoid overclaiming
- give a strong preview of the report

Try not to lean on the same contrast frame you use in the introduction. If both open with a “while things look fine, the findings suggest otherwise” setup, vary one of them.

---

## 7. Write the full draft

Write clear chapter-based copy that:

- opens in a way that suits the chapter, not a fixed formula
- follows with evidence
- explains meaning where meaning genuinely helps
- stays easy to scan
- keeps momentum from chapter to chapter

### In each chapter

Include:

- a clear heading
- a short framing paragraph
- one or more standout statistics
- supporting explanation where useful
- subgroup or comparison detail where it adds depth
- a short observation only where it earns its place

### Style guidance

- lead with the finding, not the process
- prefer simple wording over formal phrasing
- keep claims tight
- avoid jargon
- vary openings, phrasing, and endings
- let some findings stand on their own
- make each section useful as a standalone read

---

## 8. Build in journalist-friendly elements

The report should help journalists lift findings quickly and accurately.

Include:

- headline stats that can stand alone
- crisp subheadings
- pull-quote-ready lines
- clear interpretation where it helps, without leaning on the same bridge each time
- memorable but measured section labels
- simple comparison wording where valid

### Good journalist-friendly phrasing should be

- evidence-led
- concise
- specific
- easy to quote
- not sensational
- still accurate if reused elsewhere

---

## 9. Add methodology and attribution

Every survey whitepaper must include a methodology section.

It should clearly state:

- who ran the research
- who commissioned it
- fieldwork dates
- sample size
- any relevant standards or codes followed
- any comparability caveats
- limitations where needed

### Attribution rules

- attribute findings to the survey
- frame them as findings from Health Shield’s commissioned research
- keep wording careful and accurate
- make sure comparisons are properly qualified

---

## 10. Add soft brand framing

Health Shield should appear as a trusted, informed guide.

Brand mentions should:

- feel relevant
- stay measured
- support the insight story
- avoid sales-heavy language
- avoid turning the conclusion into a brochure

### Good places for brand presence

- opening context
- expert or spokesperson contributions
- final thoughts
- About Health Shield
- regulatory and attribution sections

### Brand behaviour to avoid

- repeated product pushing
- direct sales language

- using the data as a hard-sell tool inside the report body

## 11. Prepare for gated asset use

The whitepaper must work well as a gated download, so the content should feel worth sharing details for.

Check for:

- a strong title

- clear chapter progression

## Constraints and guardrails

These rules are strict.

### Research and claims

- **Do not describe findings as nationally representative**
- **Do not present survey findings as universal fact**
- Always frame findings as **survey findings**
- Do not imply causation unless it is clearly supported
- Do not overstate subgroup differences
- Do not smooth over caveats that change meaning

### Competitor and market references

- **No competitor references**
- Do not compare Health Shield to named competitors
- Do not use implied competitor criticism as a narrative device

### Advice and regulated language

- **Avoid legal, medical, or regulated-advice framing**
- Do not sound as though the report gives legal advice
- Do not sound as though the report gives medical advice
- Do not position recommendations as regulated or formal advice

### Brand and promotion

- **Product mentions must stay soft and non-promotional**
- Do not let product references lead the story
- Tell through insight, not sales language
- Keep Health Shield’s role helpful, measured, and relevant

### Style and natural flow

- **Avoid formulaic and mechanically assembled writing.** Do not repeat the same sentence formulas across the report. Use constructions like “By X, organisations can Y” and “not just X, but Y” no more than once each. Vary your openings, transitions, and section endings. The report should read as if a person wrote it with care, not as if sections were built from a fixed kit.
- **Do not force a business case onto every finding.** Lead with human significance where that is the truer point, and save commercial framing for findings where it genuinely fits.
- **Use observation boxes sparingly**, not after every chapter.
- Do not lean on the same opener more than twice across the whole report.

### Tone and evidence

- favour **quotable, evidence-led phrasing without sensationalism**
- avoid alarmist wording
- avoid sweeping national statements
- avoid unsupported “crisis” language unless it is clearly supported and approved

### Required regulatory wording

The following wording must appear **exactly as written** in the final asset:

`This is a Financial Promotion`

`Health Shield Friendly Society Limited is authorised by the Prudential Regulation Authority and regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority and the Prudential Regulation Authority.`

Do not paraphrase this wording in final output.

## Preferred phrasing patterns

These are a starter set, not a script. Rotate them, and do not use any single one more than twice in a report.

Use phrasing like:

- “Our survey found…”
- “The findings suggest…”
- “Employees surveyed said…”
- “The research highlights…”
- “The data points to…”
- “Among those surveyed…”
- “These findings indicate…”
- “People told us…”
- “Respondents described…”

Avoid phrasing like:

- “This proves…”
- “The nation believes…”
- “UK employees are…”
- “Everyone is facing…”
- “This shows employers must…”
- “Businesses should…” when it sounds like formal advice

Use sparingly, once each at most:

- “By X, organisations can Y”
- “not just X, but Y” and “not only… but also…”
- “This matters because…”
- “What this shows is…”
