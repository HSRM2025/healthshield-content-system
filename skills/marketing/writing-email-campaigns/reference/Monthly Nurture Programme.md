# Monthly Nurture Programme

## Purpose

This file sets the standard for Health Shield monthly nurture emails. It explains how to plan and write recurring wellbeing emails in a consistent way across audiences.

Use this reference when creating monthly educational emails for prospects, brokers, and customers. It covers strategy, structure, tone, and guardrails. It does not cover one-off promotional campaigns or product launch emails.

## Scope

This reference applies to monthly nurture emails that:

- share practical wellbeing content
- point readers to a useful resource
- support relationship-building over time
- use one clear primary CTA
- may link to a seasonal theme or awareness moment

This reference does not apply to:

- urgent operational emails
- product launch emails
- sales-only campaigns
- member claims or policy communications
- clinical or medical advice content

## Primary audiences

### Prospects

Prospect emails should lead with helpful content and practical value. They should show how Health Shield supports employers who want to look after their people.

Use this audience when the goal is to build trust, stay relevant, and keep Health Shield front of mind.

### Brokers

Broker emails should frame the content as something useful to share with clients. The value is twofold: it helps the broker support clients and reminds clients of the wider value of a health cash plan.

Use this audience when the goal is to support broker relationships and give them ready-to-share wellbeing content.

### Customers

Customer emails should focus on added value within the existing relationship. They should help customers make more of their health cash plan or wider wellbeing support offer.

Use this audience when the goal is to deepen engagement, support retention, and keep wellbeing visible across the year.

## Programme goals

Monthly nurture emails should aim to:

- share helpful, easy-to-use wellbeing content
- keep Health Shield visible in a warm and useful way
- support employers with practical ideas for their people
- help brokers support clients with shareable resources
- help customers get more value from their existing relationship
- connect wellbeing themes to workplace support where relevant

These emails should inform and support first. They should not feel like hard-sell marketing.

## Required inputs

Collect these inputs before writing:

- **Audience**: prospect, broker, or customer
- **Month or send window**: for example, April or May
- **Monthly theme**: for example, social wellbeing, gut health, financial wellbeing
- **Featured resource**: toolkit, tipsheet, guide, checklist, webinar, or similar
- **Resource title**: the exact name of the asset
- **Primary message**: the main point the reader should take away
- **Business goal**: nurture, engagement, retention, broker support, or awareness
- **Primary CTA**: download, share, read, explore, or similar
- **Seasonal or awareness hook**: if relevant
- **Proof points or key takeaways**: what the resource includes
- **Compliance notes**: any approved wording limits or claim restrictions

## Recurring email structure

Use this structure for each monthly nurture email.

### 1. Subject line

Keep it short, clear, and useful. Lead with the value of the resource or the relevance of the theme.

### 2. Preheader

Add supporting detail that builds on the subject line. Do not repeat it word for word.

### 3. Opening

Start with a simple, relevant introduction. This can mention:

- the monthly theme
- a seasonal moment
- a common workplace need
- why the topic matters now

Keep this section brief.

### 4. Main message

Introduce the resource and explain why it is useful. Focus on practical support, not promotion.

Where helpful, include a short list of what the resource covers.

### 5. Relevance or value section

Explain why the content matters for this audience now. This might include:

- helping teams feel supported
- making wellbeing easier to talk about
- giving clients a practical resource to share
- adding value to an existing plan or relationship

### 6. CTA section

Use one clear next step only. The CTA should match the reader’s role and the asset type.

### 7. Sign-off

Keep the close warm, simple, and supportive.

## Audience adaptation rules

### Prospects — value-led nurture framing

For prospects, focus on support and relevance. Show that Health Shield understands the needs of employers and can help them create a healthy, happy workplace.

Do:

- lead with practical help
- connect the topic to employee wellbeing
- show understanding of employer challenges
- keep product references light and relevant

Do not:

- push for a hard sale
- overload the email with product detail
- make the email sound like a brochure

### Brokers — shareable client-facing framing

For brokers, present the resource as something they can pass on to clients. Make it easy for them to see how the content helps strengthen client relationships.

Do:

- say clearly that the resource can be shared
- frame the email as practical support for clients
- link the content to the value of employee benefits where relevant
- keep the copy professional, clear, and easy to forward

Do not:

- write as if the broker is the end user of the resource
- use employer-only framing with no broker angle
- make the CTA about buying rather than sharing

### Customers — existing-relationship or added-value framing

For customers, focus on helping them get more from the relationship they already have with Health Shield. The tone should feel supportive and useful.

Do:

- frame the content as added value
- link the topic to everyday wellbeing support
- show how the resource can help their people
- refer to the health cash plan where relevant and approved

Do not:

- write as if the customer is a cold lead
- rely on broad product claims
- overstate likely outcomes

## Monthly theme selection

Monthly themes should feel timely, relevant, and easy to act on. They can be shaped by:

- awareness days or awareness weeks
- seasonal wellbeing needs
- recurring workplace wellbeing topics
- themes already planned in the campaign calendar
- available approved resources

Choose themes that:

- are easy to explain in plain English
- connect naturally to workplace wellbeing
- have a clear practical angle
- support Health Shield’s role as a helpful guide

Good examples include:

- social wellbeing
- gut health
- stress awareness
- sleep
- financial wellbeing
- inclusion and belonging
- self-care
- movement and everyday health

## Resource types

Monthly nurture emails should usually lead to one practical resource. Common formats include:

- toolkit
- tipsheet
- guide
- checklist
- downloadable PDF
- webinar or short event
- article hub or resource page

Choose a resource that is:

- easy to describe in one or two lines
- relevant to the monthly theme
- suitable for the audience
- ready to support a single clear CTA

## CTA rules

Each monthly nurture email must have one primary CTA.

### CTA principles

The CTA should:

- use a clear action verb
- match the audience’s role
- fit the asset type
- feel helpful, not pushy

### Suitable CTA examples

- Download the toolkit
- Get the guide
- Read the tipsheet
- Explore the resource
- Share the toolkit

### CTA rules by audience

- **Prospects**: usually download, get, explore, or read
- **Brokers**: usually share, pass on, or explore for clients
- **Customers**: usually download, share with your team, or explore

Avoid vague CTA wording such as:

- Click here
- Learn more
- Submit

unless the context makes the action fully clear.

## Tone and messaging rules

Monthly nurture emails should sound:

- warm
- caring
- clear
- supportive
- practical
- trustworthy

### Writing rules

- Use plain English
- Keep paragraphs short
- Use bullet points where they help
- Focus on the reader’s needs before any product mention
- Sound human and informative, not corporate
- Keep the message calm and useful

### Messaging rules

- Lead with practical value
- Tell, do not sell
- Keep product mentions secondary to the content
- Link the topic to workplace wellbeing where helpful
- Make the reason for the email clear within the first few lines

## Compliance and health-content guardrails

These emails may cover health and wellbeing topics, so the language must stay careful and clear.

### Must do

- Keep content educational and general
- Use non-clinical wording
- Say a resource is designed to help or support, not guaranteed to fix
- Make sure any health-related point is suitable for a broad audience
- Follow approved product names and brand terms

### Must not do

- diagnose
- prescribe
- suggest the content replaces medical advice
- promise results or outcomes
- make unapproved claims about prevention, treatment, or recovery
- imply that one resource will improve health for everyone

### Safer wording examples

Use:

- can help support
- practical advice
- simple steps
- designed to help
- may help people feel more supported

Avoid:

- will improve
- cures
- prevents
- guarantees
- fixes

If a claim could affect legal, regulatory, or medical risk, remove it or send it for review.

## Send logic guidance

Monthly nurture emails should follow a steady, predictable rhythm. They are part of an ongoing programme, not a one-off push.

### General guidance

- Send once per month per audience stream
- Tie the send date to the campaign calendar where possible
- Use a seasonal or awareness hook only if it adds clear relevance
- Keep one theme per email
- Keep one resource per email where possible

### Audience logic

- **Prospects**: use to build trust over time with helpful, relevant content
- **Brokers**: use to give them useful material they can share with clients
- **Customers**: use to reinforce value and keep wellbeing support visible

### Exit or suppression guidance

Do not send if the contact:

- has unsubscribed
- is outside the intended audience segment
- would receive duplicate versions of the same monthly email
- should be excluded under current campaign or contact rules

## Common failure modes

Watch for these issues when writing or reviewing monthly nurture emails:

- the email sounds too sales-led
- the topic is too broad or unclear
- the audience is not obvious
- the same copy is reused across all audiences
- the broker version does not feel shareable
- the customer version reads like prospecting
- the health language sounds clinical
- the copy implies medical advice or guaranteed outcomes
- there is more than one CTA
- the resource is named but not explained
- the awareness hook feels forced or irrelevant
- the message is too long for a simple monthly send
