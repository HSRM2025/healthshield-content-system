# Friendly Language Standard

**Governance area:** Voice and tone\
**Applies to:** All member-facing and customer-facing content\
**Status:** Active

---

## Purpose

This standard defines how Health Shield writes – and how it doesn't. It exists to make sure every piece of content we produce sounds like us: warm, human, and easy to understand. Not like a terms and conditions document. Not like a letter from a distant corporation. Like a genuinely helpful friend who knows their stuff.

Use this standard alongside `shared/voice.md` and `shared/style.md`.

---

## Swap formal for friendly

We're a warm, personable bunch here at Health Shield, and we don't want to sound like some distant, impersonal entity. So let's keep our language simple, relatable, and free from corporate jargon.

When writing for our members, assume a reading age of 9–11. That doesn't mean dumbing things down – it means making things clear. Short sentences. Plain words. Ideas presented one at a time.

A good way to test this is to read your draft aloud. Does it sound like something you'd naturally say to someone? If it doesn't, the words below are probably the reason why.

## A note on sentence starters

You can start a sentence with *but*, *and*, *so*, or *because*. There is no rule against it, and doing so makes writing sound more natural and conversational. It's how people actually talk.

**Example:**

> We've updated your allowances. But if anything looks off, just get in touch and we'll sort it out.

That reads naturally. "However, if anything appears inaccurate" does not.

---

## Why this matters

Formal language puts distance between us and the people we care about most – our members. It can make simple things feel complicated, and complicated things feel impossible.

Our members are dealing with everyday healthcare costs, claims, and decisions about their wellbeing. They need information that's easy to take in, not content that needs decoding.

Clear, friendly writing isn't just a style preference. For Health Shield, it's part of how we support people.

---

## Applying this standard

**Always:**

- Choose the friendly word when a formal alternative exists in the glossary above
- Read drafts aloud before finalising – if you wouldn't say it, don't write it
- Aim for short sentences and short paragraphs
- Write to a person, not at an audience

**Never:**

- Use jargon to sound authoritative
- Pad sentences with formal filler ("in order to", "it is important to note that")
- Use the formal column words in member-facing content without good reason

**When in doubt:** ask yourself, *would a trusted friend say this?* If yes, you're on the right track.
