## About the report

The Britain at Work Report 2026 is Health Shield's annual employee research publication. It is based on a nationally representative survey of 2,002 UK employees, conducted by Censuswide between 27 March and 1 April 2026.

The report captures employee views on workplace wellbeing, NHS access, cost of living pressures, absenteeism, and the value of health benefits. It includes year-on-year trend data, expert commentary, and Health Shield editorial content.

It is a published, approved document. All statistics and findings within it have been through a review and sign-off process and may be used in content with proper care and attribution.

## Caution points

### This is employee research, not universal proof

The report reflects the views and experiences of 2,002 employees surveyed at a specific point in time. It is a strong and credible source, but it does not represent every employee, every sector, or every organisation. Do not present findings as absolute facts about all workers.

### Year-on-year comparisons need care

The 2025 and 2026 survey samples may differ in composition. Some variation in results may reflect methodology, not fundamental shifts in employee sentiment. Do not treat year-on-year changes as definitive proof of a trend without acknowledging this.

### Sentiment and behaviour are not the same

Some findings reflect what employees feel or believe, such as concern about the cost of living. Others reflect what they report doing, such as cancelling dental appointments. These are related but distinct. Keep that distinction clear in your writing.

### Do not extrapolate beyond the data

If the report shows a correlation, write it as a correlation. Do not turn it into a guarantee or a causal claim. For example: the data shows a link between feeling supported and lower absence rates. You can use that. You cannot say that any specific product or benefit will deliver a specific outcome for a specific employer.

### Younger employee data needs extra care

Several findings show pronounced differences for employees aged 18 to 27. Use this data thoughtfully. Avoid language that could feel stigmatising or that overstates the vulnerability of any group.

## Claim handling guidance

Before using a finding in any content output, check that:

- the statistic appears in `key-statistics.md` or can be verified directly in the full report
- you are quoting it accurately and in context
- your wording reflects what the data actually shows, not what you would like it to show
- you have not combined or rounded figures in a way that changes their meaning
- any health or workplace outcome framing uses careful language

### Preferred wording for claims

Use phrasing that attributes findings clearly and keeps claims measured:

- "our research found that..."
- "according to the Britain at Work Report 2026..."
- "survey findings show\..."
- "53% of employees surveyed said..."
- "the data suggests..."
- "findings indicate..."

### Wording to avoid

Do not use phrasing that overstates certainty or generalises beyond the data:

- "employees always..."
- "every employer faces..."
- "this proves that..."
- "health cash plans will reduce absence by..."
- "the evidence shows that \[product] guarantees..."

## Health and workplace outcome claims

The report is used in content that often touches on health, healthcare access, and absence. These areas carry specific risk under Health Shield's legal and safety standards.

When using report findings in health or outcome-related content:

- use words like "may", "can help", "can help support", or "designed to help"
- do not promise specific health improvements as a result of any benefit or product
- do not present general wellbeing research as clinical evidence
- do not say a product or service will prevent illness, cure conditions, or guarantee productivity gains
- do not use report findings to imply that Health Shield products have been medically proven

Refer to `shared/legal-safety.md` for the full set of prohibited patterns and safer wording alternatives.

## Attribution guidance

All published content that cites this report must include a clear source attribution. Use the following:

**Full citation:**

> Health Shield Friendly Society. (2026). *Britain at Work Report 2026: The employee view on workplace wellbeing.* Published May 2026.

**Short in-copy attribution:**

> According to Health Shield's Britain at Work Report 2026...

**Stat-level attribution:**

> 89% of employees surveyed said they would be more likely to stay with an employer that offers health and wellbeing benefits (Britain at Work Report 2026, Health Shield).

Do not attribute findings to Censuswide unless you are specifically referencing the research methodology. The report is a Health Shield publication.

## Do and do not

### Do

- check `key-statistics.md` before quoting any figure
- attribute every statistic and finding to the report
- keep claim language measured and contextual
- distinguish employee sentiment from reported behaviour
- apply `shared/legal-safety.md` rules to all health and outcome-related content
- use the report to support and ground content, not to overstate what it proves

### Do not

- invent, round, or combine statistics in a way that changes their meaning
- use findings to make specific product outcome promises
- present survey opinion as established fact
- ignore the year-on-year methodology caveat when writing trend content
- use the report in content that includes clinical, diagnostic, or treatment claims
- reproduce large sections of the report verbatim without checking with the Content and Brand team
