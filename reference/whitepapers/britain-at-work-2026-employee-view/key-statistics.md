# Britain at Work Report 2026: key statistics

**Purpose:** Quick-reference file for content creators and AI agents working within the Health Shield Content System. Use these statistics when writing marketing content, thought leadership, employer communications, sales enablement, or agent-generated outputs that draw on Health Shield's published employee research.

**Source:** Britain at Work Report 2026: The employee view on workplace wellbeing. Published May 2026 by Health Shield Friendly Society.

**Full citation:**\
Health Shield Friendly Society. (2026). *Britain at Work Report 2026: The employee view on workplace wellbeing.* Published May 2026.

---

## Methodology snapshot

- **Sample:** 2,002 UK employees
- **Data collection:** 27 March to 1 April 2026
- **Commissioned by:** Health Shield Friendly Society
- **Research agency:** Censuswide (member of the Market Research Society and British Polling Council; signatory to the Global Data Quality Pledge)
- **Conducted in accordance with:** MRS Code of Conduct and ESOMAR guidelines
- **Year-on-year note:** Some variation between the 2025 and 2026 findings may reflect differences in sample composition or methodology, not fundamental shifts in employee sentiment. Treat year-on-year comparisons accordingly.

## Approved key statistics by theme

### General wellbeing and work-life balance

- 72% of employees report feeling happy and motivated at work.
- 73% rate their current work-life balance as good or very good.
- 40% say work frequently or always interferes with their personal or family life.
- 30% identify high workloads as the main barrier to a healthy work-life balance.
- 31% report occasional interference between work and personal commitments.
- 44% of male employees report frequent work-life interference, compared with 31% of female employees.

### Flexible working

- 74% of employees are offered flexible working options.
- Among those offered flexible working, 81% report a positive impact on their health and wellbeing.
- 20% of employees have no access to flexible working, despite a clear preference for it.
- 78% of men are offered flexible working, compared with 72% of women.

### Feeling valued and supported

- 88% of employees say their employer supports their health and wellbeing.
- 38% feel highly valued and supported by their employer.
- 42% feel valued but see room for improvement.
- 20% do not feel valued or supported at all.
- Employees who feel happy, motivated, and highly supported take an average of 8.7 sick days per year. Those who do not feel happy, motivated, or supported take an average of 12.3 days per year.

### Access to NHS healthcare

- 53% of employees say NHS waiting times or access to treatment have affected their ability to work in the last year.
- 22% say these delays have had a significant impact on their ability to work.
- 55% have experienced difficulty getting a GP appointment.
- 48% have experienced delays in follow-up care after a GP referral.
- 35% say a lack of healthcare access has caused them personal suffering.
- 30% have missed work entirely due to a lack of access to healthcare.
- 44% have attempted to self-manage a health condition, driven by limited access to professional care.
- In 2025, 62% of employees reported difficulty booking a GP appointment. In 2026, this figure improved to 55%.

### Health conditions and their impact at work

- 43% of employees have a physical or mental health condition that affects their ability to work at their best.
- This rises to 59.1% among employees aged 18 to 27.
- 14% say their health condition significantly impairs their daily work performance.
- 29% say it impacts them slightly.

### Absenteeism

- Employees take an average of 9.5 days off work per year due to ill health, mental health issues, and physical problems. This compares with 7 days in 2025.
- Mental health is a significant driver of absence, particularly for workers under 44.
- Employees take an average of 3.3 days per year to care for a family member.

### Retention risk

- More than one in three employees have considered reducing their hours or leaving work entirely due to a health-related issue.
- 43% of employees under 35 have considered reducing their hours or leaving employment because of health challenges.

### Cost of living and financial wellbeing

- 80% of employees express concern about the current cost of living. This compares with 78% in 2025.
- 72% report worrying about their finances on at least a weekly basis.
- 47% say they are more worried about the cost of living than they were the previous year.
- Financial anxiety is particularly high among women (83%) and part-time workers (82%).
- 75% of employees have cut back on routine healthcare spending, such as optical and dental care, due to the cost of living.
- 33% have cancelled healthcare appointments, such as dental check-ups, to save money.
- 34% have skipped meals to manage costs.
- 57% have relied on savings or credit to cover everyday expenses.

### Impact of financial pressure on productivity

- 48% of employees believe the rising cost of living has affected their ability to perform at work.
- This rises to over 70% among employees aged 18 to 27.
- 69% of employees believe employers should take a more active role in supporting financial wellbeing, beyond direct pay.

### Employee health benefits and what people want most

- 76% of employees say support with healthcare costs is the most important benefit to them.
- 72% highly value access to a counselling or support helpline.
- 70% consider a mental wellbeing app a vital tool.
- 74% identify improved access to physiotherapy as critical to reducing absence.
- 75% believe faster access to medical diagnostics or treatment would help them return to work more quickly.
- 82% believe accelerated access to diagnostic tests would support a faster recovery.
- 73% of employees say they are confident using digital services to access routine healthcare.

### Retention and loyalty

- 89% of employees say they would be more likely to stay with an employer that offers health and wellbeing benefits. This figure is consistent with 2025 findings.
- 79% of employees actively looking for a new role say the availability of health and wellbeing benefits is an important factor when evaluating prospective employers.

### Responsibility for employee health

- 80% of employees agree they have a personal responsibility to actively manage their health to stay fit for work.
- Younger employees (18 to 27) suggest the most balanced split of responsibility: 29% attributed to employees and 29% to employers.
- The perceived responsibility of employees increases with age, while the perceived role of government decreases.

## How to use these statistics

### Use them as evidence, not as proof

These figures reflect the views and experiences of 2,002 UK employees surveyed in early 2026. They are a strong indicator of workforce trends, but they should not be presented as universal facts about every employee or every organisation.

### Keep wording measured

Prefer language such as:

- "our research found that..."
- "survey findings show\..."
- "according to the Britain at Work Report 2026..."
- "53% of employees surveyed said..."

Avoid wording that overstates certainty or extrapolates beyond what the data supports, for example "employees always" or "every employer will face".

### Distinguish sentiment from behaviour

Some statistics reflect what employees believe or feel (for example, 80% are worried about the cost of living). Others reflect reported behaviour (for example, 33% have cancelled dental appointments). Keep this distinction when writing. Sentiment and behaviour are related, but they are not the same.

### Do not turn correlations into guarantees

For example, the data shows a link between feeling supported and lower absence. You can communicate this as a finding. Do not present it as a guaranteed outcome for any specific employer.

### Follow Health Shield's legal and safety standards

When using these statistics in health-related or product-related content, apply the rules in `shared/legal-safety.md`. Preferred wording includes "may help", "can help support", and "designed to help". Avoid outcome guarantees such as "will reduce absence" or "prevents ill health".

### Always attribute the source

Use the full citation when referencing findings in published content:

> Health Shield Friendly Society. (2026). *Britain at Work Report 2026: The employee view on workplace wellbeing.* Published May 2026.

For shorter in-copy attribution, use:

> According to Health Shield's Britain at Work Report 2026...
