# Style

**Governance area:** Style and formatting\
**Applies to:** All content outputs across all channels and formats\
**Status:** Active\
**Owner:** Content and Brand team\
**Review cycle:** Annual

---

## Purpose

This file sets out Health Shield's style rules. It applies to everything we write, across every channel, for every audience. Following these rules means our content is consistent, accessible, and unmistakably ours.

Use this file alongside:

- `shared/voice.md` – tone attributes and usage guidance
- `shared/legal-safety.md` – required disclosures and compliance rules
- `governance/friendly-language-standard.md` – formal-to-friendly word substitutions
- `governance/consumer-duty-standard.md` – FCA Consumer Duty content requirements

## Scope

**Applies to:**

- All member-facing, employer-facing, and public-facing content
- Marketing, UX copy, emails, web, documents, and social media
- AI-generated and human-written content produced under the Health Shield Content System

**Out of scope:**

- FCA-mandated statutory wording where phrasing cannot be altered
- Internal technical documentation where no audience relationship applies

---

## Core principles

Before applying any specific rule, these principles apply:

- **Plain English first.** If a simpler word exists, use it.
- **Write for the reader.** Member-facing content should be comfortable to read for someone with a reading age of 9 to 11.
- **Short is good.** Short sentences, short paragraphs, one idea at a time.
- **Active voice, not passive.** "We'll sort this out" beats "This will be sorted out."
- **Warm and human.** Read every draft aloud. If it sounds stiff or corporate, it needs work.

## Capitalisation

### Default: sentence case

Sentence case is the default for all content. Capitalise only the first word of a sentence and proper nouns. Do not capitalise words simply because they seem important.

**Example:**

> "Helping employers keep their teams healthy" ✓\
> "Helping Employers Keep Their Teams Healthy" ✗

### Members: always capitalised

**Health Shield capitalises "Members" as a deliberate brand and stylistic choice.** Members come first. Capitalising the word reflects that commitment and is consistent across all content.

**Examples:**

> "Our Members are at the heart of everything we do." ✓\
> "Contact our members team for help." ✗

This applies whether "Members" appears mid-sentence or at the start.

### Product names: title case

All official Health Shield products use title case. Do not capitalise general references to the same type of product.

| Referring to a product specifically | General reference             |
| ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Corporate Health Cash Plan          | health cash plan              |
| Select Health Cash Plan             | a cash plan                   |
| Employee Assistance Programme (EAP) | employee assistance programme |
| MyWellness benefits                 | wellbeing benefits            |
| Welcome Pack                        | welcome pack                  |

**Examples:**

> "We offer a range of health cash plans." ✓\
> "Ask about the Corporate Health Cash Plan." ✓\
> "Ask about the Corporate health cash plan." ✗

### Company name

Always write **Health Shield** as two separate words. Never write it as "Healthshield" or "HEALTH SHIELD."

### Friendly society

Only capitalise when referring to the specific legal entity: **Health Shield Friendly Society**. In all other uses, write "friendly society" in lower case.

> "Health Shield Friendly Society Limited is authorised by the Prudential Regulation Authority..." ✓\
> "As a friendly society, we don't have shareholders." ✓

### Website section names and navigation

Use title case for named website sections and navigation labels.

> "Member Resources", "About Us", "Contact Us" ✓

### Headlines and subheadings

Use sentence case unless the heading is a formal website section name.

> "Managing your team's health plans" ✓\
> "A closer look at the Corporate Health Cash Plan" ✓

### CTAs and buttons

Sentence case only. Keep them conversational and clear.

> "Find your health cash plan" ✓\
> "Download your guide" ✓\
> "Find Your Health Cash Plan" ✗

## Punctuation

### Em dash: banned

**Do not use the em dash (—) for sentence breaks, parenthetical interruptions, or emphasis.** It is not part of Health Shield's style.

If you need to create a pause, introduce a clause, or add emphasis, use one of these instead:

| What you need                         | Use this                             |
| ------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| A natural break between clauses       | A comma                              |
| A complete new thought                | A full stop and a new sentence       |
| Genuine parenthetical emphasis (rare) | An en dash with a space on each side |

**Examples:**

> "We'll help your people stay healthy, and that matters to us." ✓ (comma)\
> "We've updated your allowances. If anything looks off, just get in touch." ✓ (full stop)\
> "This service – available 24/7 – connects you with a qualified GP." ✗ (en dash with spaces, banned)\
> "This service—available 24/7—connects you with a qualified GP." ✗ (em dash, banned)

### En dash

The en dash (–) may be used sparingly where a comma or full stop would feel too abrupt. Always put a space before and after it.

> "Our plans are flexible – you can choose the level of cover that works for you." ✓\
> "Our plans are flexible–you can choose the level of cover that works for you." ✗

### Hyphens

Use hyphens only to join compound words or modifiers where needed for clarity.

> "NHS-approved app" ✓\
> "step-by-step process" ✓\
> "well being" → write as "wellbeing" (no hyphen needed)

Never use a hyphen where a dash is needed.

### Commas

You can use a comma before "and" if it creates a natural pause or prevents confusion in a list (the Oxford Comma is acceptable).

> "We cover dental, optical, and physiotherapy costs." ✓

### Exclamation marks

Use exclamation marks **only in D2C content** (direct Member communications, member emails, and community content like Breeze). Do not use them in B2B content, client communications, or prospective client materials.

> "Your new Member Account is here!" ✓ (member email)\
> "We're delighted to offer this service!" ✗ (client brochure)

### Colons

Use a colon to introduce a list. Do not capitalise the first word after a colon unless it is a proper noun.

> "Your plan covers the following: dental, optical, and physio." ✓

### Full stops

Use full stops at the end of full sentences in body copy. Do not use full stops in button labels, short CTAs, or headings.

## Grammar

### Contractions

Use contractions. They make our writing feel natural and warm. Avoid the expanded form unless you need emphasis.

| Use    | Avoid                            |
| ------ | -------------------------------- |
| We're  | We are (unless for emphasis)     |
| You've | You have (unless for emphasis)   |
| We'll  | We will (unless for emphasis)    |
| Thanks | Thank you (in informal contexts) |

### Sentence starters

You can start a sentence with "And", "But", "So", or "Because". It is conversational and natural. There is no rule against it.

> "Businesses come in all shapes and sizes. So do our health cash plans." ✓

### Active voice

Default to active voice. It is clearer and more direct.

> "We'll sort this out quickly." ✓\
> "This will be sorted out quickly." ✗

### Split infinitives

Avoid split infinitives where you can do so naturally. But if avoiding one makes a sentence awkward, the split infinitive is fine.

> "We want to quickly resolve this" → prefer "We want to resolve this quickly" where it reads naturally.

### Microcopy and error messages

Keep microcopy positive and helpful. Where you can, turn a negative into a constructive message.

> "For more tips on managing your health costs, sign up to our emails." ✓\
> "You haven't signed up to our emails." ✗

## Numbers and dates

### Numbers

Write numbers one to nine as words. Use numerals for 10 and above.

> "We have three plans available." ✓\
> "We have 12 plans available." ✓\
> "We have 3 plans available." ✗ (for numbers under 10)

Use numerals when writing percentages, measurements, currency, and specific data.

> "Up to 25% savings on gym memberships." ✓\
> "Results within five working days." ✓

### Fractions

Spell out fractions in text.

> "Five-eighths" not "5/8"

### Dates

Write dates in British English: day first, then month, then year. Do not use ordinal indicators (st, nd, rd, th) after the day number in formal content.

> "20 January 2026" ✓\
> "January 20th, 2026" ✗

In informal or digital content, shortened formats are acceptable.

> "20 Jan 2026" ✓

### Currency

Use £ immediately before the numeral, with no space.

> "£25 per month" ✓\
> "£ 25 per month" ✗

## Formatting

### Paragraphs

Keep paragraphs short. Three sentences is the ideal maximum. In digital formats, two sentences is often better. Long paragraphs are hard to scan and off-putting on screen.

### Bullet points

Use bullet points to break up lists and key details. Use a colon to introduce a bullet list. Keep each bullet item consistent in structure.

> "Your plan covers:
>
> - dental check-ups and fillings
> - eye tests and glasses
> - physiotherapy and sports massage"

Do not use full stops at the end of bullet items unless the items are complete sentences that need them for clarity.

### Headings

Use headings to break up content and help readers navigate. Keep them descriptive and plain. Sentence case as default.

### Tables

Use tables to compare options, summarise benefits, or present structured data. Keep table headers short and clear.

### Bold text

Use bold to highlight the most important word or phrase in a section. Do not use it for decoration or general emphasis. Use it sparingly so it retains its impact.

### Italic text

Italics are rarely needed in Health Shield content. They may be used for titles of publications or for occasional necessary emphasis. Do not use italics in place of bold for key information.

### Emojis

Use emojis sparingly and only in appropriate contexts:

| Use emojis              | Do not use emojis            |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Member emails           | Marketing materials          |
| Social media            | Website copy                 |
| Informal internal comms | Client documents and reports |

## Accessibility-minded style choices

- **Reading level.** Member-facing content should be readable at a 9 to 11 year-old reading age. Use short sentences, common words, and simple structure.
- **Left-align body text.** Centred body copy is harder to read.
- **Avoid ALL CAPS.** It is harder to read and can feel aggressive.
- **Link text should describe the destination.** "Find out more about health cash plans" is better than "Click here."
- **Avoid directional language.** "See the section above" can confuse readers using assistive technology.
- **Plain English for health and financial terms.** If a technical term is unavoidable, explain it immediately in plain language.
- **Sentence case in forms and labels.** It is easier to scan than title case.

## Natural writing and anti-formula rules

Health Shield content must sound natural, specific, and written by a real person for a real audience. Do not rely on formulaic patterns that make writing feel generic, automated, or over-processed.

### Avoid contrast-by-negation formulas

Do not use structures such as:

- "isn't just X, it's Y"
- "not only X, but Y"
- "it isn't merely X, but rather Y"

These patterns often sound forced and over-written. If a stronger point matters, state it directly.

Prefer:

- "This guide explains what employers need to do next."
- "Our cover helps with everyday health costs and gives people more support."

Avoid:

- "This isn't just a guide, it's a roadmap for healthier teams."
- "It isn't merely a benefit, but a commitment to employee wellbeing."

### Avoid stock transitions

Do not overuse sentence-opening transitions such as:

- moreover
- furthermore
- additionally
- consequently

These can make copy sound stiff and generic. Use them only when they genuinely help the reader follow a complex argument. In most Health Shield content, a simpler sentence or cleaner paragraph break will read better.

Prefer natural flow over formal signposting.

### Avoid buzzwords and inflated phrasing

Do not use dramatic or inflated wording where plain English will do.

Avoid phrases such as:

- delve into
- underscores
- testament to
- game-changer
- navigate the complexities
- unlock
- revolutionise
- transformative

Prefer direct, useful wording that says exactly what the reader gets.

Prefer:

- "explains"
- "shows"
- "helps"
- "important"
- "useful"
- "practical"

### Avoid rhetorical kicker lines

Do not use dramatic conversational pivots such as:

- "The kicker?"
- "Here's the truth."
- "The reality is..."
- "But here's the thing."

These often feel staged rather than helpful. If a point matters, make it plainly.

### Avoid ask-then-answer structures unless they are genuinely useful

Do not default to a pattern where the copy asks a question and answers it immediately.

Avoid:

- "Why does this matter? Because..."
- "What does this mean for employers? It means..."
- "So what can you do next? You can..."

Use this structure only when it clearly improves scannability, such as in a real FAQ. In most cases, a direct statement is stronger.

Prefer:

- "This matters because it affects how quickly employees can access support."
- "Next, choose the plan that fits your team."

### Avoid formulaic conclusions

Do not end pieces with generic summary cues such as:

- "In summary"
- "Ultimately"
- "In conclusion"

These endings feel predictable and add little value. End by reinforcing the main point, clarifying the next step, or closing naturally.

### Do not rely on em dashes for rhythm or emphasis

Em dashes are banned in Health Shield style. Do not use them to create a dramatic pause or stitch together clauses for effect. Use a full stop, comma, or rewrite the sentence.

### Vary sentence length naturally

Avoid writing where every sentence has a similar length, shape, or weight. That creates a flat and mechanical rhythm.

Aim for natural variation:
- some short sentences
- some longer ones where detail helps
- occasional fragments only where appropriate and clear

Read the draft aloud. If every sentence lands with the same cadence, rewrite.

### Avoid generic moral endings

Do not finish simple content with inflated or unearned statements such as:

- "Embrace the future of wellbeing"
- "The future starts here"
- "Together, we can transform health support"

Close in a way that feels earned, specific, and useful. A good ending should match the actual substance of the piece.


## Prohibited style patterns

The following are not permitted in Health Shield content.

| Prohibited                                                                 | Reason                                                                          |
| -------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Em dash (—) for breaks or interruptions                                    | Not part of Health Shield's style. Use commas or full stops.                    |
| "Healthshield" or "HEALTH SHIELD"                                          | Incorrect formatting of the brand name                                          |
| Formal corporate language (see `governance/friendly-language-standard.md`) | Conflicts with our warm, plain-English voice                                    |
| Overcapitalised CTAs ("Find Your Health Cash Plan")                        | Sentence case only for buttons and labels                                       |
| Passive voice as a default                                                 | Active voice is clearer and more direct                                         |
| Exclamation marks in B2B or client content                                 | Reserved for D2C Member communications only                                     |
| Long unbroken paragraphs of body copy                                      | Breaks readability. Max three sentences per paragraph.                          |
| Jargon without explanation                                                 | Readers should never need to decode our content                                 |
| Lowercase "members" when referring to Health Shield Members                | Members come first. Always capitalise.                                          |
| "Staff", "human resources", or "talent" for employees                      | Use "people" or "employees" depending on context                                |
| Contrast-by-negation phrasing ("isn't just X, it's Y") | Feels formulaic and over-written | State the stronger point directly |
| Heavy use of sentence-opening transitions ("Moreover", "Furthermore", "Additionally") | Creates a stiff, generic rhythm | Use simpler sentence links or paragraph breaks |
| Buzzword-heavy phrasing ("game-changer", "delve into", "testament to") | Sounds inflated and AI-like | Use plain, specific language |
| Rhetorical kicker lines ("The kicker?", "Here's the truth") | Feels staged and unnatural | Make the point directly |
| Ask-then-answer structure outside genuine FAQs | Creates formulaic flow | Use direct statements |
| Formulaic conclusions ("In summary", "Ultimately", "In conclusion") | Predictable and low-value | End with a relevant point or next step |
| Uniform sentence rhythm throughout a passage | Sounds flat and mechanical | Vary sentence length naturally |
| Generic inspirational endings | Feels unearned and vague | Close with a specific, grounded ending |
