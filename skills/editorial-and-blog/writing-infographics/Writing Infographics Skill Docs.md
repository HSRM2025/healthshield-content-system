# Writing Infographics

## What this skill is for

This skill helps you write the copy for an infographic. Not the design, not the icons – the words that sit in each panel and carry the story.

Infographics work best when they let the numbers do the talking. Your job is to pull the strongest figures from an authoritative report, give each one room to breathe, and link them together so the reader walks away with one clear message.

Use this skill when someone asks for infographic copy, data visualisation text, or a "stat-led" summary of a report.

## Who you're writing for

The default audience is employers, HR teams, people managers and benefits managers.

These readers are busy and sceptical. They want evidence, not sales talk. They're scanning, so every panel needs to land on its own – even if it's the only one someone reads.

Keep the tone warm, supportive and clear. We're a friendly society, not a corporate machine. Lead with the reader's challenges, then show how support can ease the pressure. Tell, don't sell.

## The core format: stat-first micro-sections

Every infographic built with this skill follows the same rhythm. Each panel has four parts:

1. **A short section heading** – names the theme (e.g. "The financial squeeze"). Sentence case. An emoji icon is fine here for digital use.
2. **One big number** – the hero stat. This is the largest text on the panel. One figure only.
3. **A short supporting line** – one sentence that explains what the number means. Bold.
4. **A secondary callout stat** – a second figure that deepens or reinforces the first, shown as a quote/callout.

That's it. Resist the urge to add more. The discipline of one big number per panel is what makes the format work.

## The golden rules

**One big number per panel.** If you have two figures fighting for attention, split them across the hero slot and the callout. Never stack three stats in one panel.

**Every figure comes from one authoritative source.** Use only numbers stated explicitly in the primary report (for example, the Britain at Work Report). No external benchmarks, no rounded-up guesses, no "industry average" stats slipped in. If it's not in the report, it doesn't go in the infographic.

**Stat first, meaning second.** Lead with the number, then explain why it matters. The reader should feel the weight of the figure before the context arrives.

**Build to a convergence point.** The panels shouldn't feel like a random list. Order them so separate pressures stack up into one bigger picture – then name that picture in a dedicated panel near the end.

**End with the employer's role, not a hard sell.** Close by acknowledging what employers can't fix, then point to the simple, caring support they can offer. Finish with a clear CTA to download the full report.

## How to write it, step by step

### 1. Read the source and pull the figures

Go through the report and list every quantitative finding. For each one, note the exact wording and the exact number. Keep a short note of where it sits so you can check it later.

Then sort your list into themes – financial, physical health, access to care, absence, retention, and so on.

### 2. Pick your panels

Choose four to six themes for the body of the infographic. For each theme, pick:

- **One hero stat** – the most striking, most reader-relevant figure.
- **One callout stat** – a supporting figure from the same theme.

If a theme only has one usable number, either pair it with a closely related figure or drop the theme. Don't pad.

### 3. Draft the panels

For each panel, write the heading, the big number, the supporting line and the callout. Keep the supporting line to one sentence. Keep the callout short.

Read it aloud. If the panel makes sense on its own, it's working.

### 4. Add the convergence panel

After the themed panels, add a panel that names the bigger story – the moment all these pressures meet. This is where you make the overriding message explicit (for example, "Four pressures. One workforce. All at once.").

### 5. Write the employer panel and CTA

Close with a short "where employers come in" panel. Acknowledge the limits, offer the hope, and list the support in plain language. Add one or two of the strongest report-backed stats about benefits and loyalty.

Finish with a clear CTA: download the full report at the relevant URL.

## Voice and style reminders

- Warm, friendly, clear. Conversational but not chatty.
- Short sentences. Short paragraphs. Plenty of white space.
- British English spelling throughout.
- Use contractions ("you're", "we've", "can't").
- No exclamation marks – this is prospect-facing content.
- Swap formal words for friendly ones: "help" not "assistance", "speedy" not "prompt", "make sure" not "ensure".
- Use en dashes – with a space either side – for emphasis.
- Lead with the reader's needs, not our products.

## What good looks like

- A reader can grasp each panel in seconds.
- Every figure can be traced back to the source report.
- The panels build to one clear, memorable message.
- The employer feels understood, not sold to.
- The CTA is obvious and easy to act on.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Cramming several numbers into one panel.
- Burying the stat under a long lead-in sentence.
- Slipping in figures from outside the source report.
- Listing themes with no connecting story.
- Ending on a product pitch instead of a helpful next step.
- Using jargon or corporate language that distances the reader.

# Rubric: Writing Infographics

Score each draft against the criteria below. Aim for "Strong" across all rows before publishing. Anything in "Needs work" should be fixed first.

---

## 1. Source integrity

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Strong** | Every figure is drawn directly from the named source report and matches its wording. No outside stats. |
| **Adequate** | All figures are from the source report, but one or two are loosely paraphrased. |
| **Needs work** | One or more figures come from outside the report, are invented, or can't be traced. |

## 2. Stat-first structure

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Strong** | Every panel leads with one big number, followed by a short supporting line and a single callout stat. |
| **Adequate** | Most panels follow the format, but one or two bury the stat or add extra detail. |
| **Needs work** | Panels mix several stats, lead with prose, or lack a clear hero number. |

## 3. One big number per panel

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Strong** | Each panel has exactly one hero figure. Secondary figures sit clearly in the callout slot. |
| **Adequate** | Mostly one figure per panel, with occasional crowding. |
| **Needs work** | Panels stack multiple competing numbers, diluting the impact. |

## 4. Story and convergence

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Strong** | Panels are ordered to build a clear story, with a dedicated convergence panel naming the bigger picture. |
| **Adequate** | A story is present but the convergence point is weak or implied rather than stated. |
| **Needs work** | Panels read as a disconnected list with no overarching message. |

## 5. Audience fit

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Strong** | Speaks directly to employers, HR and benefits managers. Leads with their challenges and what they can do. |
| **Adequate** | Generally relevant to the audience, but slips into generic or employee-facing framing in places. |
| **Needs work** | Misjudges the audience or focuses on the wrong concerns. |

## 6. Brand voice

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Strong** | Warm, supportive and clear. Conversational but respectful. Uses preferred friendly words and contractions. |
| **Adequate** | Mostly on-voice, with a few formal or jargon-heavy phrases. |
| **Needs work** | Cold, corporate, or salesy. Breaks voice rules (e.g. exclamation marks in prospect content). |

## 7. Employer panel and CTA

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Strong** | Acknowledges limits, offers caring support in plain language, and ends with a clear report-download CTA. |
| **Adequate** | Includes support and a CTA, but the framing leans towards a product pitch. |
| **Needs work** | Hard sell, vague support, or a missing/unclear CTA. |

## 8. Readability and style

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Strong** | Short sentences, short panels, British English, en dashes, no jargon. Easy to scan. |
| **Adequate** | Mostly readable, with the odd long sentence or style slip. |
| **Needs work** | Dense, hard to scan, or littered with style and spelling errors. |

---

## Quick pass/fail checks

Before anything else, confirm:

- [ ] No figure appears that isn't in the source report.
- [ ] No panel carries more than one hero number.
- [ ] There is a clear convergence panel.
- [ ] The CTA names the report and a URL.
- [ ] No exclamation marks in this prospect-facing copy.

# Examples: Writing Infographics

This file shows the skill in action. The worked example below is the Britain at Work Report 2026 infographic copy. Every figure is drawn from that report only.

---

## Worked example: Britain at Work Report 2026

**Brief:** Tell the story that multiple pressures are converging on employees in 2026. Audience: employers, HR teams and benefits managers. Stat-first micro-sections, one big number per panel, warm and supportive voice, ending with a clear report-download CTA.

---

# Britain at Work Report 2026

## Four pressures. One workforce. All at once.

In 2026, your people aren't facing a single challenge – they're facing several together. Here's what the report reveals.

---

## 💷 The financial squeeze

# 80%

**Of employees worry about the current cost of living.**

> **72% worry about their finances on a weekly basis.**

---

## 🩹 Healthcare on hold

# 75%

**Of employees have cut back on routine healthcare spending – like dental and optical care.**

> **33% have cancelled key appointments, such as a dental check-up.**

---

## ⏳ Waiting longer for care

# 53%

**Of employees say NHS waiting times have affected their ability to work.**

> **55% have struggled to even book a GP appointment.**

---

## 🤒 Rising absence

# 9.5 days

**The average number of sick days taken per year – up from 7 in 2025.**

> **43% have a health condition affecting their ability to work at their best.**

---

## ⚠️ The convergence point

# 1 in 3

**More than 1 in 3 employees have considered cutting their hours or leaving work because of their health.**

> **48% say the cost of living has affected how well they can do their job.**

---

## ✅ Where employers come in

You can't fix the cost of living or NHS waiting lists. But you can give your people simple, caring support that eases the pressure where it counts.

> **76% say support with healthcare costs is the most important benefit to them.**

> **89% would be more likely to stay with an employer that offers health and wellbeing benefits.**

When your people feel supported, your whole business feels the difference.

**Download the Britain at Work Report 2026 at healthshield.co.uk** – and see what's really weighing on your team.

---

## Why this example works

**Source integrity.** Every figure – 80%, 72%, 75%, 33%, 53%, 55%, 9.5 days, 43%, 1 in 3, 48%, 76%, 89% – comes straight from the Britain at Work Report. Nothing external slipped in.

**One big number per panel.** Each themed panel leads with a single hero stat. The second figure always sits in the callout slot, so the reader's eye never has to choose between two competing numbers.

**Stat first, meaning second.** The big number lands before the explanation. The reader feels the figure, then learns what it means.

**A real story, not a list.** Financial pressure, healthcare on hold, NHS waits and rising absence each take a turn – then the convergence panel pulls them together into one message: these pressures arrive at the same time.

**Employer-focused close.** The final panel acknowledges what employers can't fix, offers caring support, and backs it with loyalty and value stats. The CTA is clear and easy to act on.

## Mini examples: single panels

These show the panel format in isolation, for quick reference.

### A clean panel
```

## 💷 The financial squeeze

# 80%

**Of employees worry about the current cost of living.**

> **72% worry about their finances on a weekly basis.**

```

Works because: one hero number, one short supporting line, one callout.

### A weak panel (before)
```

## Money worries

# 80%

**80% of employees worry about the cost of living, 72% worry weekly, 57% expect it to get worse and the UK economy loses billions a year to stress.**

```

Problems: four numbers crammed together, one from outside the report, the hero stat lost in the crowd.

### The same panel (after)
```

## 💷 The financial squeeze

# 80%

**Of employees worry about the current cost of living.**

> **72% worry about their finances on a weekly basis.**

```

Fixed: trimmed to one hero stat and one report-backed callout. The external "billions lost" figure is removed because it isn't in the source report.

---

## A note on adapting this example

When you reuse this format for a different report:

- Swap in your own source and its figures.
- Keep four to six themed panels, plus a convergence panel and an employer panel.
- Re-check every number against the new source before publishing.
- Keep the voice warm, clear and supportive throughout.
