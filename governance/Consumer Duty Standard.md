**Governance area:** Compliance and content standards\
**Applies to:** All member-facing, customer-facing, and employer-facing content\
**Status:** Active\
**Owner:** Content and Brand team\
**Review cycle:** Annual, or following FCA regulatory updates

## Purpose

This standard sets out how Health Shield content teams apply the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)'s Consumer Duty regulations to everything we write and publish. It sits alongside our brand voice guidance to make sure we stay warm and human while meeting our legal obligations.

The FCA expects us to communicate in a way that is clear, fair, and not misleading. That's not at odds with who we are. In fact, it fits Health Shield's values well. We're a friendly society that genuinely cares about the people we support. Good Consumer Duty practice is just good communication.

Use this standard alongside `shared/voice.md`, `shared/legal-safety.md`, and `governance/friendly-language-standard.md`.

---

## Consumer Duty

The FCA's Consumer Duty sets a higher standard for how financial services firms communicate with customers. For Health Shield, this means every piece of content we create needs to actively support our members and clients – not just avoid misleading them.

The five principles below shape how we put this into practice.

---

### 1. Layering

**Give the most important information first. Then build on it.**

Members and clients need to understand the key facts before anything else. Lead with what matters most to them – what they can do, what it costs, what happens next. Then link to more detail for those who need it.

If action is needed – like making a claim before a deadline, or reviewing their allowances – make that clear and tell them what happens if they don't act.

**In practice:**

- Open with the most relevant information for that audience at that moment
- Use a clear call to action where a decision or next step is needed
- Link to supporting detail rather than including everything upfront
- Be honest about consequences of inaction where that's relevant

---

### 2. Engaging

**Design content that draws people in and makes key information easy to find.**

Dense blocks of text are easy to skip. Use formatting to help members and clients take in the information they need without having to work hard for it.

**In practice:**

- Use headings to signpost what each section covers
- Use bullet points to break up lists and key details
- Bold or highlight the most important points
- Use tables where you're comparing options or summarising benefits
- Keep paragraphs short – three sentences is ideal

The goal is content that feels approachable, not content that looks thorough on paper but is hard to use in real life.

---

### 3. Relevant

**Only include what this audience, at this moment, actually needs.**

Not every piece of content needs to explain everything about our plans. Think about who you're writing for, what decision they're making, and what they need to know to make it confidently.

**Ask yourself before writing:**

- What does this person need to understand by the end?
- Are there any areas where confusion is likely?
- Am I including information that belongs in a different communication, at a different stage?

Irrelevant information doesn't just waste space – it can actually make important details harder to find.

---

### 4. Simple

**Plain language isn't a style choice. For Health Shield, it's a standard.**

Wherever possible, avoid jargon and technical terms. If a term is unavoidable – for example, where FCA-mandated language applies – explain it clearly in plain English straight after.

Our member-facing content should be accessible to someone with a reading age of 9–11. That doesn't mean over-simplifying. It means being clear.

**In practice:**

- Follow the formal-to-friendly word substitutions in `governance/friendly-language-standard.md`
- Present information in a logical order
- One idea per sentence where possible
- Read drafts aloud – if it sounds awkward, simplify it

---

### 5. Well-timed

**Reach people with the right information at the right moment.**

Timing matters. A member who has just joined needs different information from a member who is about to renew. Someone making their first claim needs a different kind of support from someone who claims regularly.

Think about where the person is in their relationship with Health Shield, and make sure the content matches that moment.

**In practice:**

- Map content to key touchpoints in the member or customer lifecycle
- Give people enough time to read, understand, and act before a deadline
- Don't overload new members with everything at once
- Follow up at natural moments – after a claim is processed, at renewal, or when a plan is updated

## Balancing brand voice with compliance

Health Shield's warm, friendly voice and the FCA's Consumer Duty requirements work together, not against each other. Being clear, fair, and helpful is exactly what our members and clients expect from us.

But there are moments where we need to include statutory disclosures or regulated language. When that happens:

- Include the required disclosure – don't omit or obscure it
- Position it where it's visible and accessible, not buried in small print
- Where possible, explain what the disclosure means in plain English alongside it
- Make sure the overall communication still feels human and supportive

If you're not sure whether a disclosure is needed, check `shared/legal-safety.md` or raise it with the compliance team before publishing.

## Application example: health cash plans

### The positioning

> *Taking the pain out of everyday healthcare bills*

This is a strong, human, empathetic line. It leads with a real problem our members face – the cost of everyday healthcare – and positions Health Shield as the solution. That's exactly the right approach.

### How to apply it in a compliant, customer-friendly way

**Strong example:**

> Dental check-ups, eye tests, physiotherapy, prescriptions – everyday healthcare costs add up quickly. With a Health Shield health cash plan, your employees can claim back these costs up to their agreed allowance, so money doesn't have to be a barrier to looking after their health.

This works because it:

- Leads with the customer's real concern (costs)
- Names specific, relatable examples
- Explains what the plan does in plain terms
- Includes an important accuracy detail ("up to their agreed allowance") without making it feel like a disclaimer
- Stays warm and benefit-focused without overpromising

**What to avoid:**

> *Claim back ALL your healthcare costs – no limits, no hassle!*

This overpromises. It implies unlimited cover and suggests a simplicity that may not reflect every member's experience. It would not meet the Consumer Duty standard for fair and not misleading.

### The rule of thumb

If a statement would need a lengthy footnote to make it accurate, rewrite the statement. Build the accuracy in from the start, and keep the tone warm.

## Mandatory requirements

The following apply to all content covered by this standard:

- **Writers shall** structure communications so the most important information comes first
- **Writers shall** use formatting – headings, bullet points, bold text – to support comprehension
- **Writers shall** write in plain English, following the guidance in `governance/friendly-language-standard.md`
- **Writers shall** match the content to the audience's stage in the member or client lifecycle
- **Writers shall** include all required statutory disclosures where relevant
- **Writers shall not** make claims about plan benefits that could mislead members about what they are entitled to
- **Writers shall not** omit or minimise information that a member or client would reasonably need to make an informed decision
