# `market-survey-whitepaper` — SKILL.md

## Purpose

Use this skill to create a **market-facing, gated, survey-led whitepaper** for Health Shield that:

- presents survey findings in a clear, credible, journalist-friendly way
- supports PR coverage and wider campaign activity
- turns validated data into a strong editorial narrative
- balances insight, warmth, and authority
- stays within compliance, brand, and regulatory boundaries

This skill is for whitepapers built around **survey results** rather than opinion alone. The output should feel polished, useful, and easy to quote, while staying careful with claims.

This skill should produce content that helps employers, partners, brokers, media contacts, and other external audiences understand the story behind the findings without drifting into hype or hard sell.

## Gold-standard model

The attached published Health Shield survey whitepaper is the **gold-standard model** for this skill.

Use it to guide:

- overall structure
- section order
- level of detail
- tone and pacing
- use of headline stats
- chapter-based storytelling
- expert commentary placement
- conclusion style
- attribution and end-matter

Do not copy wording unless instructed. Do mirror the **quality, shape, and level of polish**.

---

## What this skill is for

Use this skill when Health Shield needs to turn survey data into a substantial external report that is:

- **market-facing**
- **gated**
- tied to a **campaign objective**
- shaped to support **press release activity**
- suitable for **PR amplification**
- written so findings are easy for journalists to lift and quote
- designed to build trust, authority, and relevance through evidence-led storytelling

Typical use cases:

- annual market reports
- workforce or wellbeing trend reports
- employer insight whitepapers
- sector pulse reports
- campaign-led research papers
- survey-based thought leadership assets

---

## When to use

Use this skill when all or most of the following are true:

- there is a completed survey with validated findings
- the asset is for an external audience
- the story needs more depth than a press release or blog post
- the report should support media outreach
- the content needs a clear narrative arc, not just a list of stats
- the report needs compliance review and marketing sign-off
- brand mentions should stay supportive and light-touch

---

## When not to use

Do **not** use this skill for:

- internal research summaries
- raw data dumps
- analyst decks
- press releases
- sales brochures
- product-led brochures
- case studies
- academic papers
- technical research reports with no editorial layer
- articles that do not depend on original survey findings

Use a different skill if the main goal is:

- internal insight sharing
- promotional product messaging
- reactive PR comments
- SEO blog content
- methodology-heavy research publication

- includes media-friendly statistics and quotable lines

- cover title

- contents page
- executive summary
- introduction
- methodology
- chapter-based findings sections

- headline stats

- expert commentary sections where available
- Health Shield conclusion or final thoughts
- citation guidance
- media contact details if required
- About Health Shield section
- regulatory wording in the final asset

### Output qualities

The whitepaper must be:

- **evidence-led**
- **clear and easy to scan**
- **credible**
- **warm but professional**
- **quotable without sounding exaggerated**
- **useful to journalists, employers, and partners**
- **soft in brand promotion**
- **structured for a gated download experience**

### Writing requirements

- use short paragraphs
- prefer clear, everyday language
- explain what the finding means, not just what it says
- make stats easy to spot and easy to repeat
- avoid loaded or dramatic phrasing
- maintain a calm, supportive, informed tone
- keep product mentions secondary to the insight story

## Decision logic

Use this logic before and during drafting.

### 1. Is there a real story here?

If the data does not support a strong, useful narrative, do not force one. Build the report around what is clearly supported.

### 2. What is most newsworthy?

Prioritise findings that are:

- surprising
- timely
- relevant to current public or employer concerns
- strongly evidenced in the survey
- easy to express in one line
- likely to matter to media and stakeholders

### 3. What is most useful to the target audience?

A media-friendly stat is helpful, but the report also needs practical meaning. Choose findings that support both:

- external interest
- reader value

### 4. What belongs in the main narrative?

Use the main chapters for findings that support the central story. Move lower-priority stats into:

- sidebars
- charts
- callouts
- supporting commentary

### 5. When should brand appear?

Brand should support the insight story, not dominate it. Mention Health Shield:

- in the introduction where appropriate
- in expert commentary attribution
- in the conclusion
- in About Health Shield
- where context requires it

Do not let the whitepaper become a sales document.

### 6. What should be quotable?

Prioritise lines that:

- summarise a clear finding
- connect evidence to real-world impact
- sound natural when lifted into press coverage
- remain accurate when quoted out of context

### 7. What must be checked again?

Always re-check:

- percentages
- year-on-year comparisons
- subgroup claims
- causality wording
- representativeness wording
- implied recommendations
- any sentence that sounds too absolute

## Workflow

## 1. Confirm the brief

Start by checking:

- campaign objective
- target audience
- desired PR value
- gating context
- timeline
- review route
- required end matter
- whether year-on-year comparison is needed

**Goal:** know what the whitepaper needs to achieve before selecting angles.

---

## 2. Review and validate the research inputs

Read and organise:

- methodology
- topline findings
- cross-tabs
- subgroup cuts
- prior-year comparisons if available
- supporting commentary inputs

Flag:

- weak findings
- findings that need context
- findings that may be easily overstated
- findings that are statistically interesting but not useful editorially

**Goal:** separate the strongest story material from background data.

---

## 3. Select the strongest media-worthy findings

Choose findings that are:

- clear
- relevant
- timely
- evidence-backed
- simple to repeat accurately
- meaningful to the audience

Look for a balanced mix of:

- headline figures
- supporting evidence
- subgroup nuance
- trend or comparison points
- business or human impact

**Do not** include stats just because they are available.

---

## 4. Build the narrative arc

Turn the selected findings into a coherent report journey.

A strong survey whitepaper usually moves through:

- the big picture
- the pressure points
- the consequences
- what people need
- what this means for organisations or the market
- a measured brand conclusion

### Narrative arc questions

- What is the central message?
- What tension or gap does the data reveal?
- Which chapters best support that message?
- What order helps the reader understand the issue clearly?
- Where should expert voices deepen the story?

**Goal:** produce a report that reads as a connected argument, not a stack of survey slides.

---

## 5. Create the chapter plan

Draft a chapter-by-chapter structure before full writing.

For each chapter define:

- working heading
- main takeaway
- supporting stats
- subgroup insights
- chart or graphic opportunities
- possible quotable lines
- whether expert commentary belongs there

### Example chapter purposes

| Chapter type             | Purpose                                 |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------- |
| State of the issue       | Set context and scale                   |
| Pressure point           | Show what is driving concern            |
| Human or business impact | Explain why the finding matters         |
| What people need         | Move from problem to response           |
| Final thoughts           | Close with calm authority and relevance |

---

## 6. Draft the executive summary early, refine late

Draft an early version to clarify direction.

Then refine it after the body copy is complete.

The executive summary should:

- capture the biggest findings
- explain why they matter
- reflect the campaign objective
- stay quotable
- avoid overclaiming
- give a strong preview of the report

---

## 7. Write the full draft

Write clear chapter-based copy that:

- opens each section with a strong point
- follows with evidence
- explains what the evidence means
- stays easy to scan
- keeps momentum from chapter to chapter

### In each chapter

Include:

- a clear heading
- a short framing paragraph
- one or more standout statistics
- supporting explanation
- subgroup or comparison detail where useful
- a short takeaway or observation if needed

### Style guidance

- lead with the finding, not the process
- prefer simple wording over formal phrasing
- keep claims tight
- avoid jargon
- make each section useful as a standalone read

---

## 8. Build in journalist-friendly elements

The report should help journalists lift findings quickly and accurately.

Include:

- headline stats that can stand alone
- crisp subheadings
- pull-quote-ready lines
- clear “what this shows” interpretation
- memorable but measured section labels
- simple comparison wording where valid

### Good journalist-friendly phrasing should be

- evidence-led
- concise
- specific
- easy to quote
- not sensationalist
- still accurate if reused elsewhere

---

## 9. Add methodology and attribution

Every survey whitepaper must include a methodology section.

This should clearly state:

- who ran the research
- who commissioned it
- fieldwork dates
- sample size
- any relevant standards or codes followed
- any comparability caveats
- limitations where needed

### Attribution rules

- attribute findings to the survey
- frame them as findings from Health Shield’s commissioned research
- keep wording careful and accurate
- make sure comparisons are properly qualified

---

## 10. Add soft brand framing

Health Shield should appear as a trusted, informed guide.

Brand mentions should:

- feel relevant
- stay measured
- support the insight story
- avoid sales-heavy language
- avoid turning the conclusion into a brochure

### Appropriate places for brand presence

- opening context
- expert or spokesperson contributions
- final thoughts
- About Health Shield
- regulatory and attribution sections

### Inappropriate brand behaviour

- repeated product pushing
- direct sales language
- overclaiming outcomes
- using the data as a hard sell tool inside the report body

---

## 11. Prepare for gated asset use

The whitepaper must work well as a gated download.

That means the content should feel worth exchanging details for.

Check for:

- a strong title
- a useful executive summary
- clear chapter progression
- enough depth to reward download
- scannable pages
- quotable highlights for promotional use
- clear end matter and attribution

The body copy should also support reuse in:

- landing pages
- email promotion
- press materials
- social snippets
- sales enablement summaries

## Constraints and guardrails

These rules are strict.

### Research and claims

- **Do not describe findings as nationally representative**
- **Do not present survey findings as universal fact**
- Always frame findings as **survey findings**
- Do not imply causation unless clearly supported
- Do not overstate subgroup differences
- Do not smooth over caveats that materially affect meaning

### Competitor and market references

- **No competitor references**
- Do not compare Health Shield to named competitors
- Do not use implied competitor criticism as a narrative device

### Advice and regulated language

- **Avoid legal, medical, or regulated-advice framing**
- Do not sound as though the report is giving legal advice
- Do not sound as though the report is giving medical advice
- Do not position recommendations as regulated or formal advice

### Brand and promotion

- **Product mentions must stay soft and non-promotional**
- Do not let product references lead the story
- Tell through insight, not sales language
- Keep Health Shield’s role helpful, measured, and relevant

### Tone and evidence

- favour **quotable, evidence-led phrasing without sensationalism**
- avoid alarmist wording
- avoid sweeping national statements
- avoid unsupported “crisis” language unless clearly supported and approved

### Required regulatory wording

The following wording must appear **exactly as written** in the final asset:

`This is a Financial Promotion`

`Health Shield Friendly Society Limited is authorised by the Prudential Regulation Authority and regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority and the Prudential Regulation Authority.`

Do not paraphrase this wording in final output.

---

## Preferred phrasing patterns

Use phrasing like:

- “Our survey found…”
- “The findings suggest…”
- “Employees surveyed said…”
- “The research highlights…”
- “The data points to…”
- “Among those surveyed…”
- “These findings indicate…”

Avoid phrasing like:

- “This proves…”
- “The nation believes…”
- “UK employees are…”
- “Everyone is facing…”
- “This shows employers must…”
- “Businesses should…” when it sounds like formal advice
