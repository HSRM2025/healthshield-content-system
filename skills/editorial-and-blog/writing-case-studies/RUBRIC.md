# Rubric

This checklist keeps every Health Shield customer success story warm, honest, and ready to share. Run it before a story goes live, and revise until it passes.

Remember, we tell the customer's story in their own words – we celebrate how they care for their people, with a little help from us. Tell, don't sell.

## Pass if all are true

### The story and structure

- Headline is warm and specific, naming the customer and the result or transformation
- The challenge → solution → "the difference it made" arc is clear and complete
- The challenge section shows the human impact on the team's health and happiness, not just a business niggle
- The solution explains how a health cash plan helped, focusing on their people's needs (tell, don't sell)
- "The difference it made" blends real staff stories with any numbers, so the human warmth shines through
- An "about the customer" intro gives industry, size, and work environment for quick scanning
- A "what's next" note looks ahead to how they'll keep caring for their team

### Voice and proof

- The customer sounds like themselves – quotes are genuine and in their own words
- At least one attributed quote appears (with name and title), woven through rather than only at the end
- Where numbers are shared, they're specific and attributed (absences, engagement, claims), or there's a clear note when metrics aren't shared
- Claims are clear, fair, and not misleading, in line with Consumer Duty
- Results stay specific to this customer – no implying every employer will see identical outcomes
- A warm, gentle CTA or next step is present (e.g. "Ready to build a healthier, happier workplace?")

### Tone and readability

- Warm, friendly, and caring throughout – respectful and informative, never stuffy
- British English spelling used throughout
- Short paragraphs (around three sentences) with bullet points to break up the text
- Reads naturally when said aloud – simple, relatable language, free from corporate jargon
- Health Shield product names capitalised correctly (e.g. Corporate Health Cash Plan when named specifically; lowercase for health cash plans in general)

### Approvals and usage

- **Stage 1 (editorial) is signed off:** draft, story angle, quotes, company name, spokesperson name and title, and metrics all approved by the customer
- **Stage 2 (asset and usage) is signed off:** final designed assets, logo, and supporting imagery all approved by the customer
- Approved channels are recorded, channel by channel (website, LinkedIn, YouTube, ad landing pages, eBooks and brochures, sales collateral, events)
- Approved repurposing rights are recorded, format by format (shorter clips, quote cards, stills, captions, one-page summaries)
- Signed release and usage form is stored centrally
- Publication log is set up, with a next review date (at least every 2 years)

## Fail if any are true

### Story and structure

- Headline is generic ("How \[Customer] uses a health cash plan") with no result or warmth
- The arc jumps straight to the solution, with the challenge missing or thin
- The challenge reads as a dry business problem, with no sense of the human impact
- The story leads with products and features rather than the customer and their people (selling, not telling)
- No "about the customer" intro or summary, so it's hard to scan
- Dense walls of text with no headings, bullets, or breathing space

### Voice and proof

- Over-polished wording that sounds like an advert rather than the customer
- No customer quotes, or quotes without attribution
- Words have been put in the customer's mouth, or quotes changed beyond what they approved
- Vague claims that can't be evidenced ("a huge improvement" with nothing behind it)
- Results that aren't plausibly linked to the health cash plan
- Overclaiming, exaggeration, or anything that could mislead or spook a reader
- Numbers used that the customer hasn't confirmed as up to date

### Tone and readability

- Formal, corporate, or jargon-heavy language that feels distant
- American English spelling, or inconsistent spelling
- Exclamation marks used in client-facing or prospect-facing copy
- Product names capitalised inconsistently

### Approvals and usage

- Story published before Stage 1 editorial approval is complete
- Final designed assets shared before Stage 2 asset and usage approval is complete
- Used in any channel the customer hasn't ticked
- Repurposed in a format the customer hasn't agreed to
- Missing customer approval on any public-facing element (name, quotes, logo, imagery, or metrics)
- No signed release and usage form stored centrally
- No next review date set, so the story risks drifting out of date

## The one thing to remember

A story only passes when it's warm, honest, and fully approved – the words signed off first, then the finished assets and exactly where they'll appear. If it's clear, fair, and something the customer feels proud of, you're good to go.
