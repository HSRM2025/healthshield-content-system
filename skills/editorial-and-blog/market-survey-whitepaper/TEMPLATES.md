# `market-survey-whitepaper` — TEMPLATES.md

## Purpose

Use these templates to build a **market-facing, gated, PR-supporting survey results whitepaper** for Health Shield.

Each template is here to help you produce sections that are:

- clear and easy to scan
- evidence-led and journalist-friendly
- warm, credible, and useful
- suitable for gated publication
- ready for PR support and wider campaign use
- varied and natural, so the report reads like a person wrote it
- aligned to Health Shield’s compliance and brand guardrails

Use these templates alongside:

- `SKILL.md`
- `RUBRIC.md`
- `EXAMPLES.md`
- `/shared/style.md`
- `/shared/voice.md`
- `/shared/accessibility.md`
- `/shared/legal-safety.md`

> [!IMPORTANT]
> These are starting frames, not moulds. If you fill every template the same way, the whole report starts to feel built rather than written. Use them to stay safe and clear, then vary the shape, openings, and phrasing so each section reads naturally.

---

## Core rules for all templates

Before you draft any section, keep these rules in mind:

- Frame findings as **survey findings**
- Do **not** describe findings as nationally representative
- Do **not** present survey findings as universal fact
- Do **not** include competitor references
- Keep product mentions **soft and non-promotional**
- Avoid legal, medical, or regulated-advice tone
- Prefer clear, quotable, evidence-led phrasing
- Avoid sensational or alarmist wording
- Make key findings easy for journalists to lift accurately
- Include the required regulatory wording **exactly as written** in the final asset

And a few habits that keep the writing human:

- **Vary your openings.** Not every section should begin the same way.
- **Vary your endings.** Some sections can simply present a finding and stop, without a summary or a business case.
- **Vary your phrasing.** If you have used a bridge like “this matters because” or “what this shows” once or twice, find a fresh way in.
- **Lead with people where that is the truer point.** Not every finding needs a workplace or business angle bolted on.

## 1. Cover and title block

### Purpose

Introduce the report clearly and give readers an immediate sense of:

- the topic
- the audience
- the year or reporting period
- the main angle of the research

This should help the asset feel worth downloading and easy to understand at a glance.

### When to use

Use for every survey results whitepaper.

### Required inputs

| Input | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Report title | Clear, specific, campaign-aligned |
| Subtitle | Optional but useful for context |
| Publication year | Usually included in title or subtitle |
| Audience angle | For example: employer view, employee view, broker view |
| Brand name | Health Shield or Health Shield Friendly Society, where it fits |

### Writing guidance

- Keep the main title short and clear
- Let the subtitle do the harder explanatory work
- Focus on the insight topic, not the product
- Keep brand presence measured
- Avoid sales-led phrasing
- Make the wording feel credible and useful
- Use wording that would still make sense if quoted in coverage

### Markdown skeleton

```
# [Report title]

## [Subtitle or explanatory line]

**Published:** [Month Year]
**By:** Health Shield
```

## 3. Executive summary

### Purpose

Sum up the biggest findings and why they matter.

This should give busy readers, journalists, and stakeholders a fast, useful overview of the report’s value.

### When to use

Use for every whitepaper. Draft early to shape direction, then refine once the report is complete.

### Required inputs

| Input | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Main story of the report | The central takeaway |
| Topline findings | The strongest 3 to 5 stats or themes |
| Campaign objective | To keep the summary focused |
| Audience relevance | Why the findings matter now |
| Any year-on-year shift | If relevant and approved |

### Writing guidance

- Open with the main issue in plain English
- Use the strongest findings early
- Explain why the findings matter, when the meaning genuinely adds something
- Keep the tone calm and evidence-led
- Write with media lift in mind
- Use bullets if that makes the section easier to scan
- Avoid overloading the summary with numbers
- Keep findings attributed to the survey or report
- Do not drift into recommendations that sound like formal advice

> [!TIP]
> If you set up the report’s tension with a “while things look fine, the findings suggest otherwise” frame here, use a different setup in the introduction. Repeating the same contrast frame in both places is a quick way to sound templated.

### Markdown skeleton

```
## Executive summary

[Report name] looks at [main issue] and what it means for [target audience].

[Open in a way that suits this report. You might lead with a single striking finding, a short piece of context, or a plain statement of the picture. Avoid defaulting to the same opener every time.]

Key findings include:

- **[Stat or finding 1]** – [Why it matters in one sentence]
- **[Stat or finding 2]** – [Why it matters in one sentence]
- **[Stat or finding 3]** – [Why it matters in one sentence]

[Optional short paragraph on a year-on-year change, a subgroup pattern, or wider context.]

[Close on a clear, measured note tied to the report theme. This can be human or practical. It does not have to be a business case.]
```

## 4. Introduction

### Purpose

Set up the report, explain why it exists, and give readers a warm, credible welcome into the story.

### When to use

Use near the front of every whitepaper, after the executive summary.

### Required inputs

| Input | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Report purpose | Why this report was created |
| Current context | Market, workforce, or sector context |
| Audience | Who the report is for |
| Health Shield perspective | Light-touch and relevant |
| Named spokesperson | Optional |

### Writing guidance

- Explain the reason for the report clearly
- Ground it in the audience’s real pressures
- Use a warm, supportive tone without sounding casual
- Keep the brand role helpful and measured
- Focus on helping the reader understand the issue
- If you use a named spokesperson intro, keep it concise and relevant
- Do not turn this into a product pitch
- Set up the report’s tension in a different way from the executive summary

### Markdown skeleton

```
## Introduction

[Name, role, organisation — optional]

This report is based on our latest survey of [audience or group], giving a clear view of [topic] in [year or period].

[Ground the report in what the audience is dealing with right now. Choose a setup that differs from the executive summary — a short observation, a question of context, or a plain statement, rather than the same contrast frame.]

We hope this report gives [target audience] useful evidence and practical insight to help them understand [topic]. By listening closely to what people told us, we can see where the biggest pressures sit, where needs are changing, and where support may make the most difference.
```

## 5. Methodology section

### Purpose

Show how the research was carried out and give readers confidence in the findings.

### When to use

Use for every whitepaper.

### Required inputs

| Input | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Research agency name | If used |
| Commissioner | Health Shield |
| Sample size | Exact figure |
| Audience surveyed | Who took part |
| Fieldwork dates | Exact dates if available |
| Standards followed | MRS, BPC, ESOMAR, or equivalent |
| Comparability caveat | If year-on-year data is included |

### Writing guidance

- Keep the wording clear and factual
- Include enough detail to support credibility
- Avoid overstating the scope of the findings
- If comparisons are used, add any caveats clearly
- Make sure the methodology supports proper attribution
- Keep this section neutral in tone

### Markdown skeleton

```
## Survey methodology

This report is based on quantitative research commissioned by Health Shield and carried out by [research agency].

### Survey design and fieldwork

- **Audience surveyed:** [Description]
- **Sample size:** [Number]
- **Fieldwork dates:** [Date range]
- **Location:** [Geography, if relevant]
- **Method:** [Online survey or other method]

[Research agency] is a member of [relevant bodies] and follows [relevant standards or codes], which helps support data quality and good research practice.

### Interpretation and comparability

[If relevant: Year-on-year comparisons are included to give context. Some variation may reflect changes in sample make-up, fieldwork conditions, or method, so please read these comparisons with care.]

The findings in this report should be understood as survey findings from this research sample.
```

## 6. Year-on-year insights section

### Purpose

Highlight meaningful changes between reporting periods without overstating them.

### When to use

Use only when there is a previous survey or comparable dataset that has been approved for comparison.

### Required inputs

| Input | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Previous period findings | Approved comparison data |
| Current period findings | Validated current data |
| Comparability note | Required if methods or samples differ |
| Key trend areas | Usually 3 to 5 high-value comparisons |

### Writing guidance

- Compare only what is valid to compare
- Focus on meaningful shifts, not every small change
- Make trend statements careful and precise
- Explain what changed, and why it matters when the meaning is real
- Add a caveat if method or sample changes may affect the comparison
- Avoid dramatic language about change unless it is clearly supported
- Vary how you introduce each trend, rather than using the same lead each time

### Markdown skeleton

```
## Year-on-year insights: [Previous year] vs. [Current year]

A look at findings from [previous year] and [current year] offers useful context on how [topic] may be changing over time.

### [Trend area 1]

In [previous year], [finding]. In [current year], [finding].

[One or two sentences on what this may suggest. Add the meaning only where it genuinely helps.]

### [Trend area 2]

[Introduce this one differently. You might open with the current figure, a short observation, or the shift itself.]

[Brief, careful interpretation.]

### [Trend area 3]

[Vary the opening again. Keep it natural.]

[Brief, careful interpretation.]

### Summary of key trends

- [Trend summary 1]
- [Trend summary 2]
- [Trend summary 3]

[Optional comparability note.]
```

## 7. Main findings chapter template

### Purpose

Present one major area of the research clearly, with a strong narrative, supporting data, and meaning where it genuinely helps.

### When to use

Use for each main findings chapter. Vary the shape from one chapter to the next.

### Required inputs

| Input | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Chapter title | Main theme of the section |
| Chapter intro point | What this section is about |
| Key stats | Main evidence |
| Subgroup findings | Optional but often useful |
| Context or meaning | Why the finding matters, if it does |
| Chart references | Optional |
| Pull-quote-ready line | Optional but encouraged |

### Writing guidance

- Open in a way that suits the chapter, not a fixed formula
- Lead with the strongest evidence or clearest point
- Keep paragraphs short
- Use subheadings to help scanning
- Explain what a finding means in practice, when the meaning adds something
- Make standout figures easy to lift into media use
- Use subgroup detail to add depth, not clutter
- Keep the tone measured and warm
- Avoid turning findings into broad claims about a whole population
- Avoid hard-sell product transitions
- Decide whether the point is human, commercial, or both, and lead with the truer one
- Let some sections close quietly, without a takeaway or a business case

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Do not build every chapter to the same challenge–implication–response shape. Let at least one or two sections break the pattern. And watch your bridges — if you have already leaned on “this matters because” or “what this shows”, find a different way through.

### Markdown skeleton

```
## [Chapter title]

[Short opening that suits this chapter. It might be a finding, a piece of context, a plain statement, or a brief human detail. Avoid the same run-up you used in the last chapter.]

### [Subheading focused on a specific finding]

[Lead sentence with the key point.]

- **[Stat 1]**
- **[Stat 2]**
- **[Stat 3]**

[Short paragraph on what the findings suggest, where the meaning genuinely helps. If the finding is strong on its own, you can let it stand.]

[Optional subgroup paragraph: Among [subgroup], the findings show [detail].]

### [Second subheading]

[Lead sentence with the key point.]

[Supporting evidence in a short paragraph or bullets.]

[Interpretation only if it adds value. Some sections can simply present the finding and move on.]

### [Third subheading, if needed]

[Open this one differently again.]

[Supporting evidence.]

[A measured close. This does not have to be a business point — lead with people where that is the truer thing to say.]

> [Optional pull-quote-ready line based on the evidence.]

[Optional chart reference placeholder: `Q[number]. [Question wording]`]
```

## 8. Observation box

### Purpose

Capture a chapter’s most important takeaway in a short, high-value format.

### When to use

Use **sparingly**. Add an observation only where a chapter genuinely benefits from a short summary, or after a strong cluster of findings.

Do not place one after every chapter. A box at the end of each section is one of the clearest signs of assembly-line writing, and it quickly makes the report feel mechanical. Trust the reader — many chapters can close without one.

### Required inputs

| Input | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Main takeaway | One key message |
| Supporting context | One or two lines |
| Evidence basis | Must reflect the chapter findings |

### Writing guidance

- Keep it short and clear
- Write in complete sentences
- Focus on what the findings suggest
- Make it quotable, but not dramatic
- Do not overstate certainty
- Do not introduce new evidence here
- Vary the wording, so observations do not all read the same across the report
- Link to business or workforce relevance only where it genuinely fits

### Markdown skeleton

```
### Observation

[One short paragraph that captures the chapter’s main takeaway in a clear, evidence-led way.]

[Optional second sentence. Add a wider relevance point only if it is the truer thing to say — it does not have to be a business case.]
```

## 9. Expert commentary section

### Purpose

Add informed perspective that helps readers interpret the findings, without replacing the survey evidence.

### When to use

Use when you have an approved contribution from:

- a Health Shield spokesperson
- an external commentator
- an industry expert
- a broker or partner voice

### Required inputs

| Input | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Contributor name | Full name |
| Contributor title | Job title and organisation |
| Commentary text or brief | Approved content |
| Relevant chapter or theme | What the commentary relates to |
| Bio summary | Optional but useful |

### Writing guidance

- Keep the commentary aligned to the report findings
- Make sure the expert adds meaning, not repetition
- Use a natural editorial voice
- Keep any recommendations measured
- Avoid language that sounds like formal legal, medical, or regulated advice
- If the contributor mentions solutions, keep them broad and non-promotional
- Add a short bio if it supports credibility

### Markdown skeleton

```
## Expert advice: [Commentary title]

**By [Name], [Job title], [Organisation]**

[Paragraph 1: What stands out in the findings.]

[Paragraph 2: Why these findings matter in practice.]

[Paragraph 3: What organisations, leaders, or stakeholders may want to pay attention to.]

[Optional paragraph 4: A measured reflection on response, support, or next steps.]

### About [First name]

[Short bio covering expertise, role, and relevance.]
```

## Notes for drafters

When in doubt:

- choose clarity over cleverness
- choose evidence over emphasis
- choose calm warmth over dramatic phrasing
- choose reader value over brand promotion
- choose variety over a consistent shape — a little unevenness reads as human

A simple test helps. Read three sections aloud, picked at random. If they sound like they were built from the same parts, vary them until they sound like something a thoughtful person would actually say.
