# Voice and Tone

## Scope

This file outlines how we communicate as Health Shield—warm, friendly, and approachable, always putting people first. It guides our voice and tone so that everyone feels included and valued in every interaction, whether they’re a member, client, broker, or employee. Use these principles across all channels and content to make sure our writing is always customer-centric and true to Health Shield’s caring spirit.

It applies to all audiences unless a skill or template gives tighter rules for a specific use case.

## Hard Rules

### Voice Definition

1. Voice is the consistent personality of the product. It does not change.
2. A voice definition must include exactly four attributes.
3. Each attribute requires:
   - One-sentence definition
   - One "this, not that" contrast
4. All content outputs must align with the defined voice attributes.

## Voice principles

Our writing should always feel warm, friendly, and welcoming—never distant or formal. We put people first, making sure everyone feels included, heard, and valued. Every message should be clear, caring, and approachable, with a focus on supporting and guiding our members, clients, brokers, and colleagues. Our commitment to inclusivity and customer-centric language is at the heart of every interaction.

- warm
- supportive
- clear
- practical
- trustworthy
- people-first
- inclusive
- non-clinical

We sound like an organisation that cares, knows its subject, and wants to help. We do not sound distant, overly formal, or sales-heavy.

## Default voice attributes

### Caring

Caring means we truly put people at the heart of everything we do. Our words make everyone feel welcome, safe, and supported—no matter their background, needs, or circumstances. We always write in a way that’s warm, friendly, and easy to understand, so our customers and colleagues feel comfortable and valued. Every interaction should show that we’re here to listen and help, treating each person as an individual and building genuine relationships based on trust and care.

**Do:**

- acknowledge real needs and pressures
- use considerate, respectful language
- focus on support and reassurance

**Do not:**

- sound cold or transactional
- treat people as leads, users, or numbers first
- use fear to drive action

### Warm

Warmth is woven into every word we write. Our tone is friendly, welcoming, and approachable—making sure everyone feels comfortable, respected, and part of a caring community. We speak openly and positively, always aiming to put people at ease and show we genuinely want to help. Our style is inclusive and customer-centric, building a sense of belonging for members, clients, brokers, and colleagues alike.

**Do:**

- write in a friendly, natural way
- use contractions where they fit
- keep the tone comfortable to read

**Do not:**

- become too casual
- use slang
- sound flippant about health or wellbeing

### Friendly

We use everyday language that is easy to read and comfortable to engage with, keeping things conversational and relatable without tipping into slang or over-informality.

**Do:**

- use plain English with contractions
- keep sentences and paragraphs short
- explain things simply

**Do not:**

- use jargon where a shorter word will do
- hide meaning behind formal language
- overload the reader with too much at once

### Professional

We are credible, clear, and respectful in everything we write, grounding our content in honest information and measured claims rather than jargon, hype, or vague reassurance.

**Do:**

- say what something is
- explain why it matters
- show what someone can do next

**Do not:**

- stay vague
- rely on abstract claims
- use filler that adds no value

### Trustworthy

We always aim to be open, honest, and easy to trust. Our words should give people confidence—using clear information, measured claims, and gentle reassurance. We want everyone who reads our content to feel included and valued, knowing exactly what they can expect and how we’re here to help. Every message should build trust and make our support feel personal, genuine, and friendly.

**Do:**

- be specific where possible
- keep claims measured
- be open about what a resource, product, or service can and cannot do

**Do not:**

- overpromise
- exaggerate outcomes
- use hype to create false urgency

## Tone modulation by context

The core voice stays the same, but the tone should shift by situation.

### Educational content

Tone: warm, friendly, and welcoming

Use a conversational style that puts people at ease, making sure everyone feels included and valued. Share advice and practical ideas with genuine care—be supportive, approachable, and always focused on helping every reader feel part of our community.

Use for guides, toolkits, tipsheets, nurture emails, and wellbeing resources.

- lead with useful value
- explain things in a steady, reassuring way
- keep the focus on support, not promotion

### Marketing and campaign content

Tone: upbeat, warm, and benefit-led, always putting people first

Use a positive, approachable style that feels friendly and supportive while focusing on the real needs of our members, clients, and brokers. Every message should be inclusive, accessible, and genuinely aimed at helping people and communities thrive.

Use for campaigns, landing pages, announcements, and email promotion.

- lead with what matters to the audience and show understanding of their challenges
- make the value and support clear from the start
- keep product or service mentions grounded, relatable, and never pushy
- write in a way that welcomes everyone in, making them feel seen and cared for

Use for campaigns, landing pages, announcements, and email promotion.

- lead with audience need
- show clear benefit early
- keep product or service mentions grounded and believable

### Support and service content

Tone: warm, understanding, and reassuring

Use for help content, issue handling, and service communications. Always communicate in a way that is easy to understand, inclusive, and considerate of individual needs. Start by listening and acknowledging how someone feels. Share information clearly, offer practical next steps, and make sure the customer knows they’re never on their own—with every message, show them they’re supported and genuinely valued by Health Shield. Avoid blame, jargon, or technical language that could feel intimidating, focusing instead on building trust and making every step comfortable for the reader.

Use for help content, issue handling, and operational communications.

- be clear about what is happening
- explain next steps simply
- avoid blame or defensive wording

### Health and wellbeing content

Tone: warm, friendly, supportive, and always approachable

Use for campaign and wellbeing content, health-related emails, and practical resource sharing. Our aim is to include everyone in the conversation, making sure every reader feels seen, heard, and valued. We talk about wellbeing in a way that’s easy to understand, never intimidating or clinical, and always focused on helping people feel comfortable and supported. Our language should guide, reassure, and encourage, avoiding anything that could feel judgemental or confusing. Every message is designed to make people feel that they're part of a caring, inclusive community with Health Shield by their side.

Use for wellbeing campaigns, health-related emails, and resource promotion.

- keep language general and accessible
- sound informed but not medical
- support without sounding alarmist or patronising

## Writing rules

### Use plain, friendly language

Choose words that feel warm, welcoming, and easy for everyone to understand. Our language should put people at ease, leaving no one behind. We always explain things simply—avoiding jargon and keeping the focus on how we can help, support, and guide. Our goal is to make every message feel friendly, approachable, and genuinely centred around what matters to our members, clients, brokers, and colleagues.

Prefer:

- help
- use
- start
- need
- get
- send
- show
- support
- clear
- up to date

Avoid:

- assist
- utilise
- commence
- require
- obtain
- dispatch
- demonstrate
- empower
- transparent
- accurate

### Keep it short

- Aim for short to medium sentences.
- Keep paragraphs brief.
- Use bullet points to break up dense copy.

### Use active voice

Use active voice to make our writing sound welcoming and approachable. By clearly saying who is doing what, we help everyone feel included and understood. This brings our support to life and lets customers know they matter to us.

Prefer:

- We’ll send your documents today.
- This guide helps employers support their teams.

Avoid:

- Your documents will be dispatched today.
- Team wellbeing can be supported through use of this guide.

### Use contractions

We use contractions to make our writing sound warm, approachable, and natural. This helps everyone feel included—like we’re talking to them as a friend or trusted partner, not as a distant organisation. Choose contractions that keep the message friendly and clear for all readers, always making sure the tone feels welcoming and customer-centric.

Prefer:

- we’re
- we’ve
- you’ll
- it’s

Do not force contractions where they sound awkward.

### Punctuation

- Use exclamation marks only when it feels genuinely friendly and welcoming, and mainly in direct-to-member or community communications. Avoid them for client or broker content, where a calm tone offers reassurance.
- Choose dashes thoughtfully to support clear, approachable sentences—never for style alone.
- Keep punctuation gentle and easy to follow, making every message feel comfortable, open, and inviting.

### Structure for readability

Whenever possible, shape your writing so it’s easy, welcoming, and inviting for everyone to read:

- start with what matters most to the reader
- highlight the value clearly and early on
- keep each paragraph focused on just one main idea
- use bullet points and headings to break up text and make details easier to find
- finish with a simple, friendly next step that feels encouraging and supportive

This helps every reader feel included, respected, and confident that we’re here to help them, no matter who they are.

- lead with the main point
- explain the value early
- keep one main idea per paragraph
- end with a clear next step if action is needed

## Audience empathy

Our writing always starts with empathy—putting ourselves in the reader’s shoes, understanding what matters most to them, and making sure they feel seen, heard, and valued. We make every message approachable and reassuring, whatever someone’s background or circumstances. By speaking directly to people and putting their needs front and centre, we aim to create a sense of belonging and genuine care, making it clear that we’re here to support them every step of the way.

### General rule

Start with what matters to them before talking about Health Shield.

### For employers and clients

- recognise pressures around people, wellbeing, cost, and time
- focus on practical support for teams
- sound helpful, not pushy

### For brokers

- make content easy to share and use
- show how it helps them support clients
- avoid writing as if they are the end user of every resource

### For members and individuals

- keep language especially simple
- avoid anything that may feel intimidating or overly technical
- make people feel supported and informed

## Guidance for wellbeing and health-related content

When we talk about health and wellbeing at Health Shield, our approach is always warm, friendly, and approachable. We want everyone to feel included and supported—never worried or left out. Our words should guide, encourage, and reassure, keeping things practical and easy to understand, so every reader feels welcome, valued, and fully part of our caring community.

At Health Shield, we put people first by offering a warm, friendly, and approachable tone in everything we do. Our aim is to help and guide, not pressure. We always focus on what’s genuinely useful and relevant for each person, making sure our calls to action feel supportive and never overwhelming. ## Credible rather than grandiose

We always aim for confidence that feels genuine and approachable—not showy or over the top. Our words should reflect a friendly, open attitude that includes everyone and puts their needs at the centre. We build trust by sharing honest information and real support instead of big claims. If we talk about value or benefits, we do it in a way that’s measured, warm, and easy for anyone to understand—so every person who reads our content knows we’re with them, not talking at them.

### Do

- make measured claims
- use proof when available and approved
- keep promises realistic

### Do not

- say best, leading, unbeatable, or revolutionary without proof
- make sweeping claims about outcomes
- use exaggerated superlatives as a substitute for substance

## Human rather than corporate

We always sound friendly, welcoming, and approachable—never stiff, cold, or distant. We want our words to make everyone feel included, heard, and supported. Our communication puts people at the heart of every message, using plain English and a style that feels as natural as speaking to a trusted friend. We aim to build real relationships by being open, customer-focused, and genuinely interested in helping people thrive—no matter who they are or how they interact with us.

### Do

- keep the tone respectful and warm

### Do not

- use corporate filler
- rely on management-speak
- write in a way that feels scripted or impersonal

- patronising or dismissive
- pushy or aggressive
- grandiose or boastful
- impersonal or bureaucratic

### Tone Boundaries

1. Tone adapts to context. Voice does not.
2. Tone shifts are constrained to:
   - **Errors**: Empathetic, solution-focused
   - **Success**: Brief, confirming
   - **Onboarding**: Patient, guiding
   - **Destructive actions**: Serious, precise
3. Tone must never be:
   - Playful during errors
   - Celebratory during destructive confirmations
   - Apologetic for routine system behavior

### Person and Address

1. Use "you" for the user.
2. Use "we" only when the product acts on the user's behalf.
3. Do not use "I" unless content is for a named persona or agent.
4. Do not use "one" as a substitute for "you."

### Formality

1. Use contractions in UI copy.
2. Do not use contractions in legal or compliance content.
3. Prohibited: exclamation points (except one per success state), emoji in product UI, slang, idioms.
4. Prohibited: passive voice (unless actor is unknown), nominalization, undefined jargon.

## Prohibited Patterns

| Pattern | Why prohibited | Use instead |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| `Oops!` | Playful during failure | State what happened |
| `We're so sorry` | Over-apologetic | State what to do next |
| `Awesome!` | Casual | `Done` or `Saved` |
| `One must configure` | Formal distance | `Configure your...` |
| `The system has determined` | Robotic | `We found` |
| `Please be advised` | Bureaucratic | State fact directly |
| `Don't worry` | Dismissive | Explain next step |
